Abstract 

Scientific papers do more than report results – they advance claims that later work supports, extends, or sometimes refutes. Yet existing methods for citation and claim analysis capture only fragments of this dialogue. In this work, we make these interactions explicit at the level of individual scientific claims. We introduce ClaimFlow , a claim-centric view of the NLP literature, built from 304 

ACL Anthology papers (1979–2025) that are manually annotated with 1,084 claims and 832 

cross-paper claim relations, indicating whether a citing paper supports , extends , qualifies ,

refutes , or references a claim as background .Using ClaimFlow , we define a new task – 

Claim Relation Classification – which requires models to infer the scientific stance toward a cited claim from the text and citation context. Evaluating strong neural models and large language models on this task, we report baseline performance of 0.78 macro-F1, highlighting that claim-relation classification is feasible but challenging. We further apply our model to ∼13 k NLP papers to analyze how claims evolve across decades of NLP research. Our analysis reveals that 63 .5% claims are never reused; only 11 .1% are ever challenged; meanwhile, widely propagated claims are more often reshaped through qualification and extension than directly confirmed or refuted. Overall ClaimFlow offers a lens for examining how ideas shift and mature within NLP, and a foundation for assessing whether models can interpret scientific argumentation. 1

Understanding this claim–claim interaction is therefore essential for understanding how a research field evolves. Yet this interaction – how claims are taken up, tested, and reshaped by later work – remains largely unexplored. NLP is a particularly interesting field to study this process. It spans computer science, linguistics, cognitive science, and social science (Manning and Schutze, 1999; Jurafsky and Martin, 2026); the field has repeatedly refined its central questions and criteria for progress (Pramanick et al., 2023). As NLP has grown, so too has the diversity of claims researchers advance: from early symbolic accounts of parsing (Winograd, 1972) to statistical alignment models (Brown et al., 1993a) to deep contextual 1

Yet despite the explosion of scientific claims (Pramanick et al., 2025), we still lack systematic tools for organizing them into a coherent record of what the field has learned. As a result, even basic questions – such as which claims emerged in a given decade, which ideas shaped later work, or which quietly lost relevance – remain difficult to answer at scale. A compelling way to understand the scientific character of NLP is therefore to move beyond papers as atomic units and instead focus on the claims they advance and how subsequent work engages with them. Each research paper introduces one or more central claims – about model behavior, properties of data and language, or assumptions about human language use – and later citations implicitly position new work relative to these claims. Such engagement may support a claim, extend it to new settings, qualify its scope, or actively refute it. If made explicit, these interactions would reveal the argumentative structure underlying the field’s development. In this paper, we make claim-level scientific interaction explicit. We introduce ClaimFlow , a claim-centric view of the NLP literature comprising of 304 ACL Anthology papers from 1979 to 

2025 (Bird et al., 2008), manually annotated with 

1,084 scientific claims and 832 cross-paper claim-level relations. Each relation captures how a claim in a citing paper engages with a claim in a cited paper, and is labeled as background , support ,

Given these inputs, the goal is to determine how the citing paper engages with the cited claim, i.e., how the cited claim is taken up, evaluated, or reinterpreted by the citing work. Figure 1 provides an overview of this setup. Recent work has shown that scientific claims and contributions can be identified from research papers using expert annotation and existing extraction methods (Achakulvisut et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022; Stammbach et al., 2023; Wadden et al., 2025). In this work, we do not introduce a new claim extraction method; instead, we focus on how claims in later papers engage with claims from prior work, and rely on existing models (Pramanick et al., 2025) when scaling our analysis beyond the annotated subset. To study whether current models can recover such claim-level relations, we develop baseline systems for Claim Relation Classification using both pretrained encoder models and large language models. Our experiments show that while models achieve reasonable overall performance on this task (e.g., macro-F1 0.78 ), errors often arise when distinguishing closely related forms of scientific engagement, such as extending a claim to a new setting versus qualifying its scope. We further apply the strongest-performing model to automatically identify claim relations at scale across 13 ,358 papers. 

ClaimFlow enables a longitudinal analysis of how claims evolve across decades of NLP research. We find that claim-level engagement is sparse and uneven, challenges are rare and short-lived, early claims shape subsequent work disproportionately, and widely propagated claims tend to be refined and reinterpreted rather than directly confirmed or rejected. 

Contributions. Our contributions are threefold: (1) we introduce ClaimFlow , an expert-annotated dataset of 304 NLP papers that explicitly links 

1,084 scientific claims across papers and labels their epistemic relations, enabling fine-grained analysis of how claims are reused, extended, or challenged (§ 3); (2) we define Claim Relation Classification , a new task that requires models to infer the stance a citing claim takes toward a cited claim given the citation context (§ 4); and finally, (3) we present the first large-scale, claim-level longitudinal analysis of NLP research, examining how scientific claims propagate, are challenged, and evolve across ∼13 k papers published between 1970 and 2025 (§ 5).

Citation Analysis. A large body of work analyzes citations as signals of influence or scholarly communication, often using graphs or bibliometric measures (Bornmann and Daniel, 2008). Moving beyond raw citation counts, prior work has proposed classifying citation intent or function, distinguishing roles such as background, use, or comparison (Teufel et al., 2006; Jurgens et al., 2018; Cohan et al., 2019). These approaches operate primarily at the level of sentences and focus on rhetorical or functional aspects of citations. In contrast, our work models epistemic relations between scientific claims, which captures scientific engagement that cannot be recovered from citation intent labels alone. 

Scientific Claim and Argument Mining. Prior work has explored scientific claim extraction, evidence identification, and argument structure modeling in research articles (Al Khatib et al., 2021; Binder et al., 2022; Flashner et al., 2025). Closely related efforts in scientific fact verification and claim checking evaluate whether individual statements are supported by evidence, often within a single document or against external sources (Wadden et al., 2020; Saakyan et al., 2021). While these approaches identify claims or argumentative components, they typically treat claims in isolation. Our work instead tracks interactions between claims across papers, explicitly modeling how later work engages with claims introduced in earlier publications. 

3 ClaimFlow : A Claim-Centric Dataset for Scientific Progress in NLP 

We develop the dataset to make claim-level scientific interaction in NLP explicit and analyzable. 

3.1 Conceptual Framework 3.1.1 What is a Scientific Claim? 

In this work, we define a scientific claim as a central, empirically testable assertion about methods, data, or phenomena that a paper advances as part of its contribution . This definition aligns with established views of scientific progress as the proposal and evaluation of testable assertions (Popper, 1959; Kuhn and Hacking, 1970; Lakatos, 1976) and with the scientific discourse in NLP (Swales, 2014). We treat claims as paper-level scientific commitments that are central to the authors’ argument. We distinguish between a scientific claim as an abstract proposition and its textual realizations in a paper. A claim text refers to a statement that expresses a claim; the same claim may be stated multiple times and in different linguistic forms across sections. In ClaimFlow ,such realizations are manually normalized into a 3single canonical claim representation, which serves as the unit of annotation and analysis. 

3.1.2 Claim Relations Definition and Scope. To capture how scientific claims interact across papers, ClaimFlow models claim-level relations. A claim relation specifies how a claim from a citing paper engages with a claim from a cited paper. Interpreting this interaction may involve examining the citation context – namely, the sentence containing the citation marker together with its immediately preceding and following sentences – as localized textual evidence linking the two papers. 

Assumption and Claim Availability. 

Conceptually, this framework assumes access to scientific claims expressed in both cited and citing papers. We treat claim identification as an upstream process and focus on how claims interact across papers, independent of how they are obtained. 

Components of Claim–Claim Interaction. 

Determining the relation between two claims involves reasoning over three sources of information: the cited claim, the citing claim, and the citation context. Each provides complementary evidence, but none is individually sufficient to determine the relation. The cited claim specifies the target of engagement but does not reveal how it is taken up in the subsequent work. The citing claim expresses the authors’ new assertion, yet its stance toward prior work may remain implicit without considering what prior work actually claimed. The citation context provides rhetorical and argumentative cues about how the cited work is discussed. However, the citation context alone may not explicitly indicate the nature of the interaction; it may simply acknowledge prior work or mention peripheral facts not directly related to the underlying claims. In ClaimFlow ,the claim relation is therefore defined between two claims, while the citation context serves as auxiliary evidence for interpreting how the claims are connected. For example, while pretrained contextual representations were initially shown to improve performance broadly (Devlin et al., 2019), later work reports that such gains are limited in low-resource settings (Hu et al., 2020). Distinguishing whether this constitutes support, extension, or qualification requires considering the cited claim, the citing claim, and the citation context together. 

Textual Grounding. When annotating claim relations, we distinguish between citing claim text , which expresses the authors’ own claim in the citing paper, and the cited claim text , which expresses the corresponding claim in the cited paper. Claim relations are defined between the underlying claims, but are grounded in these textual realizations within the citing paper. A citing claim may occur anywhere in the citing paper and is not necessarily located within the citation context itself. In ClaimFlow , each claim pair is associated with a citation context – defined as the sentence containing the citation marker together with its immediately preceding and following sentences – which provides localized textual evidence for interpreting the interaction between the claims. When multiple citation contexts refer to the same cited paper, the dataset associates the claim pair with the citation context that expresses the interaction between the citing and cited claims most directly. 

Relation Taxonomy. We propose a relations taxonomy that is grounded in the theoretical framework of argumentation (Toulmin, 2003) and scientific discourse analysis (Lauscher et al., 2018). Concretely, we define five claim-level relations: support , extend , qualify , refute ,and background . These relations capture whether a citing paper reinforces a claim, generalizes it to new settings, narrows its scope, contradicts it with opposing evidence, or references it without substantive evaluation. Importantly, these relations are defined with respect to individual claims, not entire papers. In Table 1, we provide a detailed description of each relation type with illustrative examples drawn from the annotated corpus.

3.2.2 Annotation 

Annotation was carried out by two NLP researchers, one of whom is an author of the paper with seven years of experience in NLP research, together with a PhD student with five years of research experience. Given the interpretive nature of claim identification and claim relations, we adopt a multi-stage annotation workflow (Teufel et al., 2006; Cohan et al., 2019). Annotation proceeds in two phases. In the first phase, annotators are provided with the abstract, introduction, and conclusion of each paper and identify claim texts , defined as statements expressing testable assertions about methods, data, or phenomena advanced by the paper. When necessary, annotators make minimal edits (e.g., resolving anaphora) to ensure that claim texts are self-contained. For each claim text, we record its originating sections. In the second phase, annotators examine papers that cite those annotated in the first phase. 4 Automatic Classification of Claim Relations 

We introduce the task of Claim Relation Classification , which operationalizes claim-level scientific interaction by predicting how a citing claim engages with a cited claim. To enable analysis beyond expert-annotated data, we rely on automatic claim identification as an upstream step; however, the primary focus of this section is the classification of epistemic relations between claims. We use ClaimFlow to benchmark multiple models on this task. 

4.1 Task Definition 

We formulate the task as a supervised multi-class classification problem that models claim-level scientific stance. Given a cited claim text hcited 

from paper B, a citation context s (defined as the sentence containing the citation marker together with its immediately preceding and following sentences) from a citing paper A, and a citing claim text hciting advanced by the citing paper, the goal is to predict the epistemic relation r ∈{support , extend , qualify , refute , background }

that characterizes how the citing claim engages with the cited claim. Formally, given an input represented as a triple (hcited , s, h citing ), the task is to predict the relation label r.

4.2 Models and Baselines 

We evaluate a range of models to assess their ability to predict claim-relations. Our goal is not to exhaustively optimize architectures, but to compare representative modeling paradigms and identify which aspects of the task current models handle well or struggle with. 

4.2.1 Encoder-Based Models 

We evaluate transformer-based encoder models as supervised baselines for claim relation classification. Specifically, we experiment with BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and RoBERTa (Liu et al.), which are widely used pretrained encoders and serve as standard reference points for sentence-and document-level classification tasks. To assess the effect of domain-specific pretraining, we additionally evaluate SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019), which is trained on scientific text. For each instance, we linearize the input triple (hcited , s, h citing ) and feed the resulting sequence into a single encoder. The final hidden representation corresponding to the [CLS ] token is passed to a softmax classifier over the relation label set. 

4.2.2 Large Language Models 

We also evaluate large language models (LLMs) in a prompt-based setting to assess whether models trained primarily for general-purpose language understanding can recognize claim-level scientific stance without task-specific fine-tuning. For proprietary models, we evaluate GPT-4o and GPT-3.5-turbo (Achiam et al., 2023). For open-source models, we evaluate LLaMA-3-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) and Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2024). All LLMs are evaluated in zero-shot and few-shot settings using a unified prompt template that presents the cited claim, citation context, and citing claim, followed by a request to select one of the predefined relation labels. 

4.3 Experimental Setup Data Splits. We evaluate all models on 

ClaimFlow using stratified train-validation-test splits constructed at the paper level to avoid leakage across citations. Papers are partitioned into 70-15-15 splits, ensuring that claims and citation contexts from the same paper do not appear in multiple splits. Label distributions are preserved across splits.

4.4 Results and Error Analysis Main Results. Table 4 shows the results. Among fine-tuned encoders, RoBERTa consistently outperforms BERT, confirming the benefit of stronger pretrained representations. SciBERT achieves slightly improved performance, suggesting that domain-specific pretraining provides modest gains. The majority-class baseline performs substantially worse, indicating that the task cannot be solved by exploiting label frequency alone. In the zero-shot setting, all LLMs perform below most of the fine-tuned encoders, whereas few-shot prompting leads to consistent improvements across models. Error Analysis. Qualitative inspection of model errors in Table 9 (in Appendix F) reveals two dominant failure modes. First, models frequently confuse extension with qualification, particularly when a citing paper applies a prior claim to a new setting while simultaneously introducing constraints. Second, models struggle with cases where support or refutation is expressed indirectly. Hedging, cautious language, and implicit comparisons can obscure epistemic stance, leading models to default to the background label. Overall, the results indicate that while current models can capture coarse scientific stance, they struggle with nuanced claim-level reasoning. 

4.5 ClaimFlow-AutoGraph : An Automatically Constructed Claim Graph 

To enable large-scale analysis beyond expert annotation, we construct an automatically inferred claim graph over a collection of 13 ,358 ACL Anthology papers using a two-stage pipeline that combines automatic claim identification and claim relation classification. In the first stage, we perform automatic claim identification at the sentence level to extract candidate claim texts from unannotated papers. Following Pramanick et al. (2025), we fine-tune SciBERT using ClaimFlow to identify sentences that express a claim, and apply the resulting model to extract claim texts in the larger corpus. Each detected sentence is treated as an individual claim text. We discuss the details of claim identification in Appendix E. In the second stage, for each citation between papers, we consider pairs of citing and cited claim texts and use GPT-4o in a few-shot setting to determine whether a citing claim engages with a cited claim and, if so, to predict the corresponding epistemic relation. When a paper cites another paper multiple times, each citation mention yields a separate citation context (defined as the citation sentence together with its immediately preceding and following sentences), and claim interactions are predicted independently for each context. This yields a directed graph in which nodes correspond to automatically identified claim texts and edges represent predicted claim-claim relations. Discussion. Results are consistent with long-standing norms in the NLP literature, where progress has typically been framed as cumulative rather than adversarial. Early symbolic and rule-based approaches (Church, 1988) were rarely rejected outright; instead, they were later positioned as foundational context when probabilistic and corpus-driven methods gained prominence (Brown et al., 1993b). Asimilar dynamic accompanied the shift from feature-engineered statistical models to neural architectures, where prior methods were generally contextualized or subsumed rather than explicitly refuted (Collobert et al., 2011). Even during major paradigm transitions, such as the move from phrase-based statistical machine translation to neural machine translation, earlier approaches were more often bounded or implicitly superseded than directly contradicted (Bahdanau et al., 2015).

To examine what happens after a claim is challenged, we identify all post-challenge interactions, i.e., claim-claim interactions occurring after the first observed challenge.

Discussion. Results show that NLP research engages with challenged claims in a limited and non-escalatory manner. The survival curve reflects the tendency toward early vetting followed by normalization. Once a claim is qualified or refuted, later work tends either to move on or to re-incorporate the claim in a modified form, rather than continuing to contest it. For example, early debates around parsing models led not to repeated rejection of specific claims (Magerman, 1995; Collins, 1997), but to shifts in evaluation regimes and modeling constraints as statistical parsing matured (Charniak, 2000). 

5.2 Claim Propagation Q3. Do claims propagate across NLP papers or remain isolated? 

For each claim h, we compute its propagation count, defined as the number of unique papers that reference h. Claims with a propagation count of zero are considered isolated , whereas claims referenced by one or multiple papers are considered 

propagated . Additionally, to analyze temporal reuse, we measure the time between a claim’s introduction and its first subsequent reuse, treating unreused claims as right-censored .

Results. In Figure 4 we observe that most claims exhibit limited propagation across NLP papers, with 63 .5% claims remaining isolated . Among claims that propagate, most are reused by only a small number of subsequent papers (mean prop. count 1.37 ), while a minority propagate widely (∼2% reused by ten or more papers). Overall, the distribution is strongly right-skewed, indicating that broad claim diffusion is exceptional rather than typical. Figure 10 (in Appendix A) further shows that claim reuse is time-concentrated. The probability that a claim has not yet been reused drops sharply within the first few years after its introduction. 

Discussion. These engagement patterns show that influential ideas in NLP have often spread through rapid early uptake followed by consolidation, rather than gradual or sustained reuse. The early decline observed in the claim survival curve reflects this historical tendency toward early selection: claims that align with emerging datasets, tools, or modeling paradigms are quickly reused, whereas others effectively exit the active discourse without explicit refutation. For example, representational shifts – such as the introduction of word embeddings and later contextualized representations – were driven by a small number of claims that achieved early community adoption and subsequently dominated reuse patterns (Mikolov et al., 2013; Peters et al., 2018), while alternative proposals introduced in the same period attracted little long-term engagement (Collobert and Weston, 2008; Turian et al., 2010).

5.3 Claim Evolution Q5. Does the claim graph densify or stratify over time in NLP research? 

For each year t, we construct a cumulative directed graph Gt = ( Vt, E t), where Vt is the set of claims introduced up to year t, and Et contains directed edges (hciting → hcited ), corresponding to claim-claim relations up to t. To assess whether the claim graph densifies over time, we compute edge density 

δ(Gt) = |Et||Vt|(|Vt|− 1) , which measures the fraction of realized claim-claim interactions among all possible directed pairs. Intuitively, higher density indicates that claims increasingly connect to many other claims, reflecting a more tightly interlinked body of work. To assess whether the graph stratifies, we measure modularity Q(Gt), which quantifies the extent to which claims form groups with dense internal connections but relatively sparse connections to other groups. Intuitively, higher modularity indicates stronger clustering of claims into distinct thematic or methodological substructures. 

Results. In Figure 5a we observe that the edge density of the claim-claim graph remains extremely low overall (on the order of 10 −3), after an initial rise in the early years attributable to small-graph effects. Despite continued growth in the number of claims and relations, density stabilizes and slightly declines over time, indicating that new claims engage with a small fraction of prior claims rather than forming broadly interconnected structures. Figure 5b shows that graph modularity increases steadily over time before stabilizing in later years. 

Discussion. As the field expanded in the 1990s and early 2000s, the emergence of large resources and standardized evaluations, such as treebanks (Marcus et al., 1993), lexical databases (Miller, 1992), and task-specific benchmarks, encouraged specialization, leading claims to cluster around distinct tasks, representations, and evaluation regimes. The subsequent plateau and recent decline in modularity are consistent with methodological convergence in modern NLP. Since the mid-2010s, contextualized and pretrained models (Devlin et al., 2019) have been widely adopted across diverse tasks, increasing cross-subfield engagement and weakening previously isolated clusters. This trend has been further reinforced by multi-task and unified benchmarks (Wang et al., 2018, 2019), which explicitly promote cross-task comparison, and by the rise of foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021), which frame many new claims as analyses or extensions of shared model families rather than task-specific innovations.

6 Discussion and Conclusion 

We introduce ClaimFlow , the first expert-annotated dataset linking claims with labeled epistemic relations, and the new task of Claim Relation Classification , that enables both model evaluation and large-scale analysis of how claims are engaged, propagated, challenged, and reshaped over time. Our findings reveal consistent structural regularities across decades and venues: most claims remain isolated, explicit challenges are rare and short-lived, early claims exert disproportional influence, and scientific progress more often proceeds through qualification and extension than outright confirmation and refutation. At scale, these dynamics produce a claim graph that remains globally sparse while becoming increasingly stratified, with recent signs of reintegration. These results challenge common assumptions that the scientific influence is broadly distributed, driven primarily by cumulative evidence, or well approximated by citation counts. Instead, we show that influence is uneven and time-dependent: early claims receive high downstream engagement even after controlling for exposure, shaping subsequent research trajectories and constraining which ideas are extended, revisited, or forgotten. Taken together, our analysis highlights that early framing choices, benchmarks, and paradigms exert lasting effects on what claims gain traction. ClaimFlow 

provides a foundation for studying scientific knowledge as an evolving system of claims, and for developing NLP systems that can reason about scientific argumentation rather than merely retrieve citations. 

7 Applications and Future Work 

ClaimFlow enables several practical and scientific applications. First, it provides a structured resource for evaluating scientific reasoning in NLP models, offering a more rigorous alternative to citation-intent or NLI tasks. Second, the citation graph can support literature navigation tools, helping researchers identify how specific claims have evolved, diverged, or been challenged over time. Third, our scientometric analyses offer new ways to track idea diffusion, detect emerging research threads, or identify claims with high impact or instability. Future work includes expanding ClaimFlow to additional venues and scientific domains, enabling cross-disciplinary comparisons of claim evolution. 11 Automatically extracting claims at scale is another promising direction, potentially bootstrapping larger claim graphs or integrating with LLM-based scholarly assistants. Finally, combining claim flow with model performance trends or robustness evaluations could support predictive analyses of research trajectories and inform community-wide assessments of methodological progress. 

Limitations 

This study focuses on NLP papers drawn from the ACL Anthology, specifically venues under the ACL Events umbrella. While these venues represent a central and influential subset of NLP research, important contributions also appear in AI Conferences, regional venues, and preprint platforms. As a result, the dataset does not capture the full breadth of scientific claims in NLP, and extending claim-level analysis beyond the ACL Anthology remains an important direction for future work. Our longitudinal analyses aggregate over claims and relations within this curated corpus. Claims may continue to evolve beyond the temporal and venue boundaries of the dataset, and some influential ideas may be underrepresented due to sampling scope. Consequently, the trends we report should be interpreted as indicative patterns within a high-quality sample rather than an exhaustive account of the field. In addition, our annotations focus on abstracts, introductions, and conclusions, where authors typically articulate their central claims most explicitly. While claims may also appear in the main body of papers, annotating full texts would require substantially greater effort. Incorporating claims from the full paper content remains a future extension of this work. Finally, our large-scale analyses rely on models trained on the human-annotated dataset to automatically identify claim relations in a broader corpus. It is important to acknowledge that no model achieves perfect accuracy, which can impact the quality of such analyses. However, Teodorescu and Mohammad (2023) showed that trend-level conclusions derived from large automatically labeled datasets tend to be highly accurate and show a strong correlation with trends identified through gold-label analysis, supporting the reliability and accuracy of our analyses. 

Ethics Statement 

In this work, we use publicly available research papers from the ACL Anthology and do not involve personal data or human subjects. All annotations were performed on scholarly text and reflect interpretations of scientific claims rather than judgments about individual authors or research communities. We acknowledge that identifying and relating scientific claims involves interpretive decisions, and that scientific progress cannot be reduced to quantitative signals alone. The analyses enabled by ClaimFlow are intended to support reflective examination of research trends, not to prescribe scientific value or replace expert judgment. Any conclusions drawn from this work should therefore be considered in conjunction with qualitative, ethical, and social considerations that shape scientific practice.

Discussion. This asymmetry suggests that NLP research exhibits a division of epistemic labor between exploration and consolidation. Most claims act as divergence points, synthesizing and recombining existing ideas to explore new modeling choices, datasets, or problem formulations. Historically, such divergence has been common during periods of rapid expansion, when researchers draw on multiple prior strands without immediately producing new focal claims – for example, during the early statistical turn, when diverse probabilistic assumptions and feature combinations were explored in parallel (Manning and Schutze, 1999). At the same time, the smaller but substantial mass of convergence-dominated claims reflects moments of field-wide consolidation, where a limited set of claims becomes shared reference points for subsequent work. Claims associated with major resources or evaluation standards – such as the Penn Treebank (Marcus et al., 1993) or widely adopted automatic evaluation metrics (Papineni et al., 2002) – illustrate this pattern, attracting sustained downstream engagement while themselves referencing relatively few predecessors. More recently, pretrained language models similarly functioned as convergence hubs, rapidly drawing engagement from across tasks and subfield (Devlin et al., 2019). Together, these findings indicate that NLP knowledge growth is not uniformly branching nor purely convergent. Instead, it proceeds through many extrapolatory, divergence-oriented claims, punctuated by periodic convergence onto a small number of stabilizing ideas. 

Q2. Do scientific claims in NLP research accumulate uncertainty over time? 

To examine whether claims accumulate uncertainty over time, we analyze the temporal trajectories of claim-claim interactions for each cited claim. We operationalize uncertainty using relations: 

qualify and refute , which respectively indicate that a claim’s scope is constrained or that it is contradicted by later work. For a claim h, we consider its sequence of downstream interactions ordered by publication year. We define the cumulative uncertainty at time 

t as the proportion of all interactions up to t that are labeled qualify and refute . We track how this proportion evolves as a function of claim age. 

Results. Figure 8 shows that cumulative uncertainty remains low throughout the claim lifecycle and is highest early in a claim’s lifecycle and decreases over time. In the first few years after introduction, a non-trivial fraction of interactions involve qualification or refutation, but as claims age, the proportion of uncertainty-inducing interactions declines, stabilizing at a low level for long-lived claims. Across all ages, uncertainty is dominated by qualification, while refutation remains rare throughout. 

Discussion. The observed decline in cumulative uncertainty suggests that uncertainty in NLP claims is front-loaded rather than cumulative. Early in a claim’s lifecycle, new claims are more likely to be tested, bounded, or occasionally contradicted as the community probes their validity and scope. As a result, claims that survive into later years tend to be those that have already passed an initial filtering process, leading to a lower proportion of uncertainty-inducing interactions over time. Historically, this pattern aligns with how influential ideas in NLP have matured. Early claims – such as those associated with new modeling paradigms or resources – often face scrutiny shortly after introduction, during which limitations are identified and scope conditions clarified. Once these boundaries are established, subsequent work typically builds on the refined claim rather than re-challenging it. Taken together, these results indicate that uncertainty in NLP operates as an early-phase corrective mechanism rather than an ongoing process of erosion. Claims either stabilize after initial qualification or fade from active use, leaving long-lived claims with comparatively low cumulative uncertainty. This dynamic helps explain why explicit refutation remains rare and 18 short-lived, and reinforces a broader picture of NLP research as progressing through early selection and path-dependent consolidation, rather than sustained adversarial debate. 

B Annotation Instructions 

This section describes the annotation guidelines provided to expert annotators for constructing the 

ClaimFlow . The goal of the annotation is to identify central scientific claims in NLP papers and to label how claims from different papers relate to one another. 

B.1 Core Definitions 

Annotators were first presented with the following definitions, which guided all subsequent annotation decisions. 

Scientific Claim. A scientific claim is a central, empirically testable assertion about methods, data, or phenomena that a research paper advances as part of its contribution. 

Claim Text. A claim text is a span of text in a research paper that explicitly expresses a scientific claim. The same underlying claim may be expressed by multiple claim texts within a paper. Annotators were instructed to distinguish between claims as abstract scientific commitments and claim texts as their textual realizations. 

B.2 Claim Identification 

Annotators were provided with the abstract ,

introduction , and conclusion of each paper and instructed to identify claim texts from these sections. For each candidate sentence, annotators answered the following question: 

Does this text express a scientific claim according to the definition above? 

A text was considered a valid claim text if it: • makes a testable assertion (rather than a definition or background statement), • concerns a method, dataset, or empirical phenomenon, • and reflects a central contribution emphasized by the authors. When necessary, annotators were allowed to make 

minimal edits (e.g., resolving anaphora or removing references such as “this model”) to ensure that claim texts were self-contained and interpretable in isolation. Annotators also recorded the section in which each claim text appeared. 

B.3 Claim-Relation Annotation 

In the second phase, annotators labeled relations between claims across papers. Annotators were shown • a cited claim text from a previously annotated paper, • a citing claim text from a later paper, • and the citation context in the citing paper (the sentence containing the citation marker together with the immediately preceding and following sentences) Annotators then answered the following question: 

Does the citing claim text substantively engage with the cited claim? 

Substantive engagement was defined as explicitly evaluating, building upon, qualifying, or challenging the content of the cited claim. When engagement was present, annotators assigned exactly one of the following relation labels: • support. The citing claim provides evidence or results that reinforce the cited claim. • extend. The citing claim generalizes or applies the cited claim to new settings, data or tasks. • qualify. The citing claim restricts the scope of the cited claim or specifies conditions under which it holds. • refute. The citing claim presents evidence or arguments that contradict the cited claim. • background. The cited claim is referenced for context or comparison without substantive evaluation. Annotators were instructed to base their decisions on the explicit content of the claim texts and citation context, not on external knowledge. 

B.4 Adjudication and Edge Cases 

Ambiguous cases – such as partial engagement, mixed signals, or unclear scope – were flagged by annotators. These cases were resolved through discussion and adjudication, with reference to the definition above. Annotators were instructed to prioritize: 19 • explicit textual evidence over implicit assumptions, • the claims under consideration rather than the overall contributions of a paper, • and conservative labeling when evidence was insufficient to justify a stronger relation. 

B.5 Notes on Scope 

Annotators were informed that the goal of the dataset is to make claim-level scientific interaction observable. As a result, the annotation focuses on clear claims and clear instances of engagement, rather than marginal or purely rhetorical references. 

C Prompting Large Language Models 

C.1 Task Framing 

Each instance consists of a cited claim text, a citing claim text, and the citation context from the citing paper. Models are instructed to select exactly one relation label from the predefined set: support , extend , qualify , refute , or 

background . Models are explicitly instructed to output only the relation label, without explanations or additional text. 

C.2 Base Prompt Template 

All LLMs are prompted using the same base template to ensure consistency across models. The prompt is shown in Figure 9. 

C.3 Decoding and Post-processing 

Models are decoded using deterministic or low-temperature settings to reduce output variability. If a model produces output that does not exactly match one of the valid labels, the output is mapped to the closest valid label when unambiguous; otherwise, the instance is marked as incorrect. No external tools, retrieval mechanisms, or chain-of-thought prompting are used.
