Abstract 

Existing scientific document retrieval (SDR) methods primarily rely on document-centric representations learned from inter-document relationships for document-document (doc-doc) retrieval. However, the rise of LLMs and RAG has shifted SDR toward question-driven retrieval, where documents are retrieved in response to natural-language questions (q-doc). This change has led to systematic mis-matches between document-centric models and question-driven retrieval, including (1) input granularity (long documents vs. short ques-tions), (2) semantic focus (scientific discourse structure vs. specific question intent), and 

(3) training signals (citation-based similarity vs. question-oriented relevance). To this end, we propose UniFAR, a Unified Facet-Aware Retrieval framework to jointly support doc-doc and q-doc SDR within a single architec-ture. UniFAR reconciles granularity differ-ences through adaptive multi-granularity aggre-gation, aligns document structure with question intent via learnable facet anchors, and unifies doc–doc and q–doc supervision through joint training. Experimental results show that Uni-FAR consistently outperforms prior methods across multiple retrieval tasks and base models, confirming its effectiveness and generality. 

1 Introduction 

With the rapid growth of scientific publications worldwide, scientific document retrieval (SDR) has become essential for effective knowledge dis-covery and scientific innovation. Dense retrieval (Karpukhin et al., 2020) has emerged as the dom-inant paradigm in information retrieval, shifting the focus from lexical to semantic matching by representing queries and documents in a shared embedding space. Models like Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022) and E5 (Wang et al., 2024) demonstrate 

> *Corresponding Author.

strong performance on general-domain bench-marks (Muennighoff et al., 2023). However, their lack of explicit adaptation to the scientific domain limits their ability in SDR tasks. To address this, a line of work has extended dense retrieval to scientific literature by exploiting inter-document relationships to capture scholarly context and structural semantics beyond isolated texts. Representative methods learn document em-beddings under citation-informed supervision to reflect scientific relatedness (Cohan et al., 2020; Os-tendorff et al., 2022), while others incorporate finer-grained signals from co-citation contexts (Mysore et al., 2022) or structured discourse components (Dou et al., 2025) to enable more nuanced seman-tic alignment. By relying on inter-document rela-tionships, these methods fundamentally follow a 

document-centric paradigm, where representations are optimized to retrieve documents given a query document (doc-doc retrieval). The development of large language models (LLMs) and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has introduced new retrieval settings centered on natural-language queries (Lewis et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2024). Recent studies have explored the use of LLMs and RAG for scientific literature, where SDR serves as a key interface between users, lan-guage models, and large-scale scientific corpora (Asai et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025). Beyond tra-ditional doc-doc retrieval, SDR is increasingly re-quired to support question-driven retrieval where scientific documents are retrieved in response to a natural-language question (q-doc retrieval). This leads to systematic mismatches between document-centric retrievers and question-driven retrieval along three dimensions. (1) Input gran-ularity : scientific documents are typically long and structured, whereas user questions are short and compressed, making document-level repre-sentations insufficient for fine-grained question matching (Cai et al., 2024). (2) Semantic focus :1

document-centric modeling emphasizes discourse structure and scientific roles (e.g., background, method, result) shared across papers (Dou et al., 2025), whereas question-driven retrieval is guided by user intent and specific information needs, caus-ing representations optimized for document similar-ity to overlook intent-relevant contents (Do et al., 2025). (3) Training signals : scientific retrievers are typically trained with citation-based supervi-sion (Cohan et al., 2020; Ostendorff et al., 2022) rather than explicitly optimizing question-oriented relevance, leading to a mismatch between training objectives and question-driven retrieval needs. Overall, document-centric approaches are effec-tive at modeling scientific relationships and struc-ture, but exhibit systematic mismatches in question-driven retrieval. This motivates the central goal of our work: Can we unify both doc-doc and q-doc retrieval for scientific literature, retaining document-level relational structure while resolving mismatches in question-driven retrieval? 

In this paper, we propose UniFAR, a Uni fied F acet-Aware Retrieval framework for scientific lit-erature that bridges document-centric modeling and question-driven retrieval within a single architec-ture. UniFAR combines adaptive multi-granularity aggregation, facet-anchored semantic alignment, and joint doc-doc and q-doc contrastive training to address mismatches in input granularity, semantic focus, and training signals. Our main contributions are threefold: 

· Unified Framework for SDR. We unify doc-doc and q-doc retrieval for scientific literature within a single framework (UniFAR) that incorporates multi-granularity representation and aggregation as well as learnable facet-level semantic anchors. 

· Facet-Aware Joint Training. We propose a facet-aware joint training strategy that integrates doc-doc and q-doc supervision within a shared semantic space, enabling unified learning for both retrieval settings. 

· Empirical Evaluation. Extensive experiments across multiple tasks and base models show that UniFAR consistently outperforms prior methods, confirming its effectiveness and generality. 

2 Related Work 

Document-Centric. A dominant paradigm in sci-entific document retrieval focuses on modeling relevance through inter-document relationships to capture scholarly context and structural seman-tics. Representative work such as SPECTER (Co-han et al., 2020) learns document embeddings via citation-informed contrastive learning to supervise doc-doc similarity. Building on this paradigm, SciNCL (Ostendorff et al., 2022) replaces binary citation supervision with neighborhood contrastive learning over citation graph embeddings, enabling graded similarity signals and more informative pos-itive and negative sampling. Multi-vector mod-els (Mysore et al., 2022) with textual guidance exploit co-citation sentences to supervise aspect-level embeddings, allowing document similarity to be computed through fine-grained semantic align-ment. FLeW (Dou et al., 2025) constructs facet-specific document training triplets based on citation intent and frequency, explicitly modeling scien-tific discourse structure via facet-level representa-tions aligned with background, method, and result. While these methods effectively enrich document representations using citation-derived signals, they remain confined to a document-centric paradigm with limited optimization for user questions. 

Question-Driven. With the growing adoption of LLM-based systems and RAG, scientific document retrieval (SDR) has become increasingly question-driven. SciRepEval (Singh et al., 2023) categorizes downstream scientific tasks to explicitly include query-based retrieval, and introduces task-aware learning to train task-specific modules. MixGR (Cai et al., 2024) integrates complementary simi-larity signals computed at different granularities, enabling more robust matching between complex scientific queries and long-form documents. FaBle (Do et al., 2025) targets faceted query-by-example retrieval by constructing facet-conditioned training data through decomposition and recomposition, al-lowing retrieval to be guided by specific semantic aspects. To improve question supervision, CCQ-Gen (Kang et al., 2025) generates concept-covering query sets for better approximation on user informa-tion needs over scientific documents. Despite these advances, question-driven approaches in SDR typ-ically remain separately modeled and supervised, rather than jointly optimizing doc-doc and q-doc retrieval within a unified framework.

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup Base Models. To evaluate the adaptability of Uni-FAR with different base models, we adopt three representative models. SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019) is a domain-specific encoder pretrained on scientific corpora, serving as a standard baseline for scientific document understanding. Contriever 

(Izacard et al., 2022) is an unsupervised dense retriever trained via large-scale contrastive learn-ing on general-domain text, representing domain-agnostic models. MPNet (Song et al., 2020) com-bines masked and permuted language modeling, providing strong general-purpose representations. 

Baseline Methods. To ensure fair comparison across different backbone encoders, we group all baselines by their underlying base models. Most prior methods are built on SciBERT for its domain specificity, including the citation-driven models SPECTER (Cohan et al., 2020) and SciNCL (Os-tendorff et al., 2022), the co-citation-based models CoSentBERT and Aspire (Mysore et al., 2022), as well as the facet-aware approaches FaBle (Do et al., 2025) and FLeW (Dou et al., 2025). For the Con-triever group, we include OpenScholar Retriever (OSRetriever) (Asai et al., 2024), which adapts con-trastive training to scientific document chunks for domain alignment. 

Benchmarks and Metrics. We evaluate Uni-FAR on both question–document (q-doc) and document–document (doc-doc) retrieval settings. For the q–doc retrieval, we evaluate on DORIS-MAE (Wang et al., 2023), a multi-level bench-mark featuring diverse queries at different semantic granularities. For doc-doc retrieval, we use CS-FCube (Mysore et al., 2021), a facet-level bench-mark where each query paper is annotated with three facets consistent with UniFAR’s facet schema. For evaluation, we use Recall@5, R-Precision, and MAP from DORIS-MAE ; and report nDCG %20 and MAP for CSFCube following prior works (Mysore et al., 2022; Do et al., 2025). Detailed descrip-tions of benchmarks and implementation settings are further provided in Appendix D and A. 

Facet-Aware Retrieval Strategies. Based on the facet similarity matrix (Section 3.3), UniFAR uses different strategies across retrieval tasks. For 

DORIS-MAE (q-doc), we compute the final similar-ity by averaging the diagonal entries of the matrix, providing an overall relevance across facets for multi-level queries. For CSFCube (doc-doc), we compute relevance using the facet-wise diagonal similarities, evaluating each facet independently under the benchmark’s fixed facet schema.

6 Analysis 

As shown in Table 3, we conduct ablation analy-ses to examine how multi-granularity aggregation, learnable facet anchors, and joint training objec-tives in UniFAR address mismatches in input gran-ularity, semantic focus, and training signals. 

Multi-Granularity Aggregation. Removing sentence-level attention (w/o MHA sent ) causes a larger drop on CSFCube (doc-doc), where long scientific documents benefit from sentence-level se-mantic structure for facet aggregation. Conversely, removing token-level attention (w/o MHA tok )leads to greater degradation on DORIS-MAE (q-doc), where short queries depend more heavily on fine-grained attention over individual tokens for facet-relevant evidence. These degradation patterns indicate that combining sentence-level and token-level attention with adaptive granularity selection is essential for maintaining robust facet embeddings across different retrieval tasks.

7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present UniFAR, a unified facet-aware retrieval framework for scientific litera-ture that jointly supports document-document and question-document retrieval. By integrating adap-tive multi-granularity aggregation, learnable facet anchors, and facet-aware joint training, UniFAR reconciles systematic mismatches between doc-doc and q-doc retrieval, including input granularity, se-mantic focus, and training signals. Extensive ex-periments across multiple benchmarks and base models demonstrate that UniFAR consistently out-performs prior approaches in both retrieval settings. Further ablation analyses confirm the contributions of key components and training objectives. We wish UniFAR can provide a fundamental and flexi-ble framework for future scientific knowledge dis-covery based on LLMs and RAG. 8Limitations 

In this work, the number of facets in UniFAR is fixed during training and shared across retrieval tasks for simplicity and stability. Nevertheless, the proposed framework itself is not restricted to a specific facet cardinality, and can naturally support different choices of Nf acet by adjusting the number of learnable facet anchors. Dynamically adapting the number or granularity of facets to different re-trieval scenarios, query complexities, or domain characteristics may further improve flexibility and retrieval effectiveness. Future work will explore leveraging facet-level representations for adaptive retrieval augmentation and knowledge grounding in scientific domain, extending the framework to-ward more interpretable and generalizable retrieval systems.

10 A Implementation Details 

We train three UniFAR variants with different base models ( SciBERT, Contriever, and MPNet ). The training corpus is constructed as described in Sec-tion 4.1, with detailed statistics and field distribu-tions provided in Appendix B. All three UniFAR variants are trained for 2 epochs using AdamW with a 5% linear warm-up, a batch size of 4, and gradient accumulation of 4 on four NVIDIA 3090 GPUs (24GB each). The contrastive temperature τ

is set to 0.08, and the KL-loss weight λ is linearly annealed from 0.3 to 0.5 during training. For SciB-ERT and Contriever , learning rates are set to 2e-5 for the base model and shared facet anchors, and 5e-5 for other aggregation modules; for MPNet , a smaller learning rate of 3e-6 is used to preserve semantic consistency of stronger base model.

11 D Benchmark Details 

DORIS-MAE (Wang et al., 2023) is designed to evaluate scientific document retrieval under com-plex, multi-intent information needs. Each query is a human-authored, long-form scientific ques-tion that is hierarchically decomposed into seman-tically distinct aspects and sub-aspects, enabling fine-grained relevance assessment beyond single-sentence queries. This multi-level structure sub-stantially increases query diversity and difficulty, and exposes the limitations of retrievers trained on simple or single-aspect queries. By explicitly mod-eling aspect-level relevance and supporting both full queries and sub-queries, DORIS-MAE pro-vides a challenging and realistic benchmark for question–document retrieval in scientific literature, making it well suited for evaluating UniFAR’s abil-ity to handle question-driven retrieval with complex semantic structure. 

CSFCube (Mysore et al., 2021) is designed for faceted query-by-example retrieval in scientific lit-erature, where each query consists of a scientific paper paired with a specific facet of interest. The dataset is constructed through expert annotation following TREC-style pooling and adjudication, producing graded relevance judgments with high inter-annotator agreement. By explicitly associ-ating relevance with rhetorical facets of scientific discourse, CSFCube enables evaluation of docu-ment–document similarity at the facet level rather than treating documents as monolithic units. This design makes CSFCube a principled benchmark for assessing whether retrieval models can capture fine-grained, facet-specific semantic alignment be-tween scientific documents, directly matching the doc-doc setting targeted by UniFAR. 

LitSearch (Ajith et al., 2024) constructs retrieval questions from in-line citation contexts and then refines them through expert validation, produc-ing information needs that closely mirror how re-searchers formulate queries during literature explo-ration. It contains both human-written and rigor-ously validated LLM-generated questions, and eval-uates retrieval over full papers rather than abstracts. These characteristics make LitSearch a demanding and practically oriented benchmark, providing a complementary setting for evaluating the general-ity of UniFAR.
