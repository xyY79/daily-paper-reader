Abstract. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) improves the reli-ability of large language model applications by grounding generation in retrieved evidence, but it also introduces a new attack surface: corpus poisoning. In this setting, an adversary injects or edits passages so that they are ranked into the Top-K results for target queries and then af-fect downstream generation. Existing defences against corpus poisoning often rely on content filtering, auxiliary models, or generator-side reason-ing, which can make deployment more difficult. We propose ProGRank, a post hoc, training-free retriever-side defence for dense-retriever RAG. ProGRank stress-tests each query–passage pair under mild randomized perturbations and extracts probe gradients from a small fixed param-eter subset of the retriever. From these signals, it derives two instabil-ity signals, representational consistency and dispersion risk, and com-bines them with a score gate in a reranking step. ProGRank preserves the original passage content, requires no retraining, and also supports a surrogate-based variant when the deployed retriever is unavailable. Extensive experiments across three datasets, three dense retriever back-bones, representative corpus poisoning attacks, and both retrieval-stage and end-to-end settings show that ProGRank provides stronger defence performance and a favorable robustness–utility trade-off. It also remains competitive under adaptive evasive attacks. 

Keywords: Retrieval Augmented Generation · Corpus Poisoning · Ro-bust Reranking 

## 1 Introduction 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) improves the reliability of large lan-guage model (LLM) applications by grounding generation in externally retrieved evidence [9, 10, 13, 16]. In practical deployments, the retrieval corpus is often assembled from web-scale or user-contributed sources rather than a fully cu-rated collection, making the corpus an additional trust boundary for the RAG  

> arXiv:2603.22934v1 [cs.AI] 24 Mar 2026 2X. Yin et al.

pipeline [5, 29, 30]. Modern RAG systems commonly rely on neural dense retriev-ers that rank passages by embedding similarity and pass the Top-K passages to the generator [14, 16, 23]. Among the emerging risks, corpus poisoning is particularly concerning. In this setting, an adversary injects or edits a small number of passages so that they are ranked into the Top-K results for target queries and subsequently steer generation through poisoned evidence [28, 38, 40]. This threat model covers rep-resentative attack families, including retrieval-optimized poisoning [38], joint op-timization with retrieval and generation objectives [40, 28], trigger-based poison-ing that activates under specific queries [2], and misleading-evidence attacks that induce unfaithful or harmful generations [4, 26]. Our goal is to reduce poisoned Top-K exposure at retrieval time, thereby lowering downstream attack success, without modifying the generator or retraining the retriever. Existing defences can be grouped by the stage at which they intervene. Generation-time defences change how retrieved evidence is consumed during decoding, for example via aggregation schemes [31], attention constraints [6], or activation-based analysis [25]. System-level approaches aim to identify and remove poisoned sources in the corpus, which often requires corpus-wide access and offline processing [36]. Retriever-side defences instead act on the ranking itself by reducing the chance that poisoned passages enter the Top-K set. How-ever, existing poisoning-mitigation methods at or around retrieval often rely on auxiliary detectors or content-based filtering [7, 17, 34], dedicated reranking pro-cedures [37], or hybrid pipelines that invoke language-model reasoning beyond retrieval [39], which can complicate deployment. We propose ProGRank, a post hoc, training-free retriever-side defence for neural dense retrievers in RAG. As illustrated in Fig. 1, ProGRank performs a lightweight stress test on each query– passage pair by computing probe gradients of the retriever similarity score with respect to a small fixed parameter subset, namely a LayerNorm module at layer 

L, under mild randomized perturbations of the retriever computation. From these probe gradients, we derive two complementary instability signals, repre-sentational consistency ( Rep ) and dispersion risk ( DR ), also denoted as the consistency penalty and the saturated risk penalty. Unlike generic uncertainty-based reranking, ProGRank probes parameter-space sensitivity rather than only representation-space variability, and combines these signals with a score gate that focuses correction on the high-score region that determines Top-K membership. The key intuition is that optimization-driven poisons can concentrate retrievabil-ity on a small set of perturbation-sensitive matching signals, which makes them more likely to yield unstable gradient responses under stochastic perturbations than benign passages. We do not assume that all poisoned passages are intrin-sically unstable, rather, we treat such instability as a useful reranking signal for poisoning mitigation. ProGRank can be used in a white-box setting with direct access to the retriever, and it also admits a surrogate-based variant for settings where the deployed retriever is unavailable. We evaluate ProGRank across multiple datasets and dense retriever back-bones [1, 14, 16, 19, 33], under representative poisoning strategies [2, 4, 28, 38, 40]. Contributions. Our contributions are three-fold: (1) We propose ProGRank, a post hoc, training-free retriever-side defence that reranks candidates using probe-gradient instability under randomized perturbations. (2) We introduce a score-gated penalty fusion mechanism that focuses robustness correction on the decision-critical high-score region. (3) We show across multiple datasets, retriev-ers, and poisoning attacks that ProGRank reduces poisoned Top-K exposure and improves downstream robustness, while remaining effective under the adaptive attack settings considered in this work. 

## 2 Related Work 

Corpus Poisoning Attacks. Corpus poisoning attacks inject or modify a small number of passages so that targeted queries retrieve them and downstream gener-ation is steered [38, 40, 28]. Existing work studies direct passage injection against dense retrievers [38], end-to-end poisoning objectives for RAG [40, 28], trigger-style poisoning [2], and retrieval corruption through low-level perturbations [4, 26]; benchmarks further show that adaptive poisoning can degrade many de-fences [35].

Positioning. ProGRank is a post hoc, training-free retriever-side defence that uses only retriever-derived signals, without auxiliary language-model inference, content filtering, or generator-side verification. Unlike filtering or hybrid verifi-cation methods [3, 17, 34, 39], it operates directly on query–passage signals from the retriever. Unlike approaches that alter retrieved content [21, 17, 3], it pre-serves the original passage content and adjusts scores in a post hoc reranking step. Unlike dedicated reranking procedures [37], it does not introduce a separate learned reranker or auxiliary model. ProGRank also admits a surrogate-based variant for settings where the deployed retriever is unavailable.

3.2 Stress Test-Based Reranking via Randomized Probing 

Traditional neural dense retrievers rank candidates using a similarity score alone. This creates an attack surface for corpus poisoning: an adversary can craft or modify passages so that they achieve high similarity with a target query and enter the Top-K results [38, 40]. ProGRank targets a practically important sub-set of such attacks, namely optimization-driven poisons whose retrievability is concentrated on perturbation-sensitive matching signals. Our key hypothesis is not that all poisoned passages are intrinsically unstable, but that this instability provides a useful reranking signal across diverse poisoning settings. As illustrated in Fig. 1, ProGRank stress-tests each query–passage pair under mild randomized perturbations, extracts gradient-based instability signals, and uses them to adjust retrieval scores in a post hoc reranking step. For each query– passage pair (q, p ), in addition to the base score in Eq. (1), we apply a stochastic perturbation operator T (·) for R runs to obtain perturbed variants pr = Tr (p)

for r ∈ { 1, . . . , R }. In our implementation, Tr (·) can be token dropout, encoder dropout, or their mixture. Token dropout randomly masks a proportion of pas-sage tokens, while encoder dropout uses the model’s internal dropout. Unless specified otherwise, encoder dropout affects the forward computation for both the query and the passage, whereas token dropout is applied only to the pas-sage. For simplicity, Tr denotes the stochastic perturbation used in the r-th run without changing the notation for q.We keep the retriever parameters fixed and probe a small fixed parameter subset ϑ ⊆ θ, instantiated in our experiments as a LayerNorm module at layer 

L. Given a perturbed passage pr , we define the probe gradient as

3.4 Mechanism-Level Intuition for Stochastic Probing 

We provide a simple mechanism-level intuition for why optimization-driven poi-soned passages can exhibit lower representational consistency (Eq. (4)) and higher dispersion risk (Eqs. (9)–(10)) under stochastic probing induced by T (·).We do not claim a formal guarantee that all poisoned passages are unstable; rather, the following abstraction explains the empirical trend observed in our experiments. The perturbation operator T (·) can be viewed as feature noising that induces stochastic representations via random masking of tokens, units, or computation paths, consistent with standard interpretations of dropout [8, 24, 27]. If a can-didate’s high similarity relies on a small set of concentrated and perturbation-sensitive features, mild perturbations can suppress their effective contribution in some runs and yield unstable gradient responses. 3.5 Towards Implementation Efficiency 

Although our formulation is defined over D, a practical deployment would in-stantiate D as a bounded first-stage candidate pool rather than the full corpus. Probe-Gradient Reranking for Defending Corpus Poisoning 11 

Fig. 6: Ablation summary across four factors: perturbation repeat R, probe layer, perturbation type, and retriever backbone. One may first retrieve a buffer of top-B candidates with B ≫ K, estimate the gate on this pool, and apply stochastic probing only to the decision-critical upper-tail candidates before selecting the final top-K. The additional defence overhead then depends mainly on the bounded pool size B, rather than directly on the full corpus size, while remaining consistent with the score-gating intuition in Sec. 3.3. 

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setups 

Datasets and corpus poisoning methods . Following the implementation in [28], we evaluate on MS MARCO [1], Natural Questions (NQ) [19], and Hot-potQA [33]. Our corpus poisoning methods include PoisonedRAG [40], LIAR-RAG [26], and Joint-GCG [28]. For each dataset, we sample 100 evaluation queries, and each query is associated with 50 clean passages. 

Retrieval-stage settings . For retrieval-stage experiments, we consider three dense retrievers: Contriever [14], DPR [16], and BGE [32]. We use a pooled-poison protocol, where all poisoned passages for the same query are added to the clean pool. For each query, the retriever returns a Top-K list with K ∈{5, 10 , 20 , 30 , 40 , 50 }. We evaluate ProGRank under four factors: perturbation repeats R ∈ { 4, 8, 16 , 20 , 24 , 28 , 32 }, probe layer L ∈ { 3, 7, 11 }, perturbation type (encoder dropout, token dropout, or their mixture), and retriever backbone. Here, L denotes the probed LayerNorm module, and for dispersion risk we fix 

τ = 0 .1, α = 4 .0, and C = 6 .0. Results in Fig. 2, 3, and 5 are averaged over these settings, while Fig. 6 reports the corresponding ablations. The ablations show that performance stabilizes around R = 20 , L = 3 performs best overall, and mixed perturbation is the strongest setting. These choices are then used in the downstream generation experiments. 4.2 Downstream Generation Analysis 

Under the downstream generation setting defined in Sec. 4.1, we evaluate Pro-GRank against the undefended baseline and compare it with representative corpus-poisoning defences, namely GRADA [37], GMTP [17], and RAGuard [18]. These baselines are selected from Tab. 1 based on code availability. Tab. 2 reports the main end-to-end robustness results. ProGRank achieves the lowest macro-average judge-based ASR on all three datasets, reaching 0.000 

on HotpotQA, NQ, and MS MARCO. It also achieves the lowest macro-average substring-based ASR on all three datasets, with 0.011 on HotpotQA, 0.000 on NQ, and 0.033 on MS MARCO. In terms of clean utility, ProGRank achieves the highest macro-average ACC on HotpotQA, while GRADA performs best on NQ and RAGuard performs best on MS MARCO. As shown in Tab. 4, RAGuard is about 20 × slower than ProGRank on average due to repeated leave-one-out veri-fication. The reported ProGRank latency is measured on the actual experimental pipeline, with all retrieved candidates reranked per query. Overall, ProGRank offers the strongest robustness and a substantially better efficiency–robustness trade-off than RAGuard in our evaluation. These results suggest that ProGRank retains robustness under the adaptive attack configurations considered in this work, especially on HotpotQA and NQ. Tab. 5 reports clean utility. ProGRank achieves the best EM on NQ, while the baseline and RAGuard perform better on HotpotQA, and GRADA gives the best ROUGE-L F1 on MS MARCO. ## 5 Conclusion 

We presented ProGRank, a post hoc, training-free retriever-side defence against corpus poisoning in dense-retriever RAG. ProGRank stress-tests query–passage pairs under mild randomized perturbations and reranks them using probe-gradient instability signals computed on a small fixed parameter subset, instantiated in our experiments as a LayerNorm module at layer L. Rather than assuming that all poisoned passages are intrinsically unstable, ProGRank targets a practically important subset of optimization-driven poisoning attacks whose retrievability relies on perturbation-sensitive matching signals. Across three datasets, mul-tiple dense retrievers, and representative poisoning attacks, ProGRank consis-tently reduces poisoned Top-K exposure and achieves the lowest macro-average judge-based ASR in end-to-end evaluation, while remaining effective under the adaptive attack settings considered in this work.
