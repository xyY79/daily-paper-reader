# Reconciling Latent Variables and Networks: Exploring and extending the Psychometric-Toolbox 

# Kevin Kistermann, Vivato V. Andriamiarana, and Augustin Kelava 

University of Tübingen (Methods Center) Abstract Since the introduction of network psychometrics, several connections to sta-tistical models in “classical” psychometrics (i.e., IRT, SEM, GLM) as well as to approaches from other research fields have been established. In this paper, these developments have been reviewed and synthesized and, based on an exploratory literature search, further advanced and presented in an accessible visual format. This perspective opens up promising opportuni-ties to extend the psychometric-toolbox by incorporating and learning from statistical methodologies developed in other research domains, which often address similar or even identical problems. Highlighting these methodolog-ical commonalities may also foster collaboration across research fields that have traditionally remained largely independent. Moreover, awareness of these connections may render methodological development more system-atic and goal-directed and may enable a meaningful division of labor, for example between the development of statistical methodology and its prac-tical implementation for empirical research through software tools. Finally, these methodological advances provide new opportunities for empirical re-search and may contribute to a reconciliation with longstanding conceptual issues concerning psychometric constructs and, more broadly, psychologi-cal phenomena. 

Keywords: network psychometrics, latent variable models, intensive longi-tudinal data, graph theory, complex dynamical systems

Initial Motivation. In recent years, the pressing necessity for more individual-level (idiographic) research in psychology has been stressed (Borsboom & Haslbeck, 2024; Chow et al., 2024; Molenaar, 2004; Piccirillo & Rodebaugh, 2019). This of course also im-plies a more dynamic picture of constructs and therefore the need for time-series data. With the increasing feasibility of Ambulatory Assessment (AA; Trull and Ebner-Priemer, 2014) protocols 1, a method for collecting Intensive Longitudinal Data (ILD; Hamaker, 2025), the need for developing appropriate statistical models to analyze such high-dimensional data is critical. Modeling frameworks for doing so have been developed in psychometrics over the last couple of years mostly in the form of dynamic latent variable models (Asparouhov et al., 2017, 2018; Kelava & Brandt, 2019). Moreover, in the last two decades, statistical models associated with "network psychometrics" (or "the network approach"; Borsboom, 2022; Borsboom, Deserno, et al., 2021; Isvoranu et al., 2022; Sweet and Wang, 2025)

Outline - (1) Exploring and extending the Psychometric-Toolbox. This paper is primarily divided into three sections. We start in section one ( Exploring and extend-ing the Psychometric-Toolbox ) by presenting the diagram 2 of relations between statistical models as the result of our exploratory literature search. Then the already established relations in psychometrics—which are spread over many publications—are accessibly out-lined, with the aim of providing a useful summary and potential starting point for subse-quent work on exploring additional relations, ideally in a more structured and thorough manner. Next, some more novel and perhaps lesser known relations—from the perspective of psychometrics—are pointed out. Some of these may bear untapped potential for ex-panding psychometric methodology in the form of model extensions and possibly enabling unifications in the form of a more general statistical model taxonomy. Lastly, introduc-tory tutorials for three models (multilevel VAR, GIMME, and Recurrence Plot /Network) are provided, analyzing a publicly available psychometric ILD dataset (from Rowland & Wenzel, 2020). 

Outline - (2) Statistical Models as Networks. In section two ( Statistical Models as Networks ) we begin by reiterating some helpful distinctions between Network Science (Sweet & Wang, 2025), Graph Theory (R. Das & Soylu, 2023; Diestel, 2025), and network psychometrics, the latter of which is—at least currently—primarily concerned with the es-timation of Probabilistic Graphical Models (Maasch et al., 2025) on psychometric datasets. We then refer to some statistical tools for modeling more than pairwise interactions as some form of covariance, which is not always appropriate (Slipetz et al., 2024). Many more sta-tistical measures are, in principle, available here (Cli ff et al., 2023). Additionally, there is an extensive literature on modeling higher-order interactions (Battiston et al., 2020) and multilayer networks (Kivela et al., 2014), which could conceivably be integrated to some extent into methodology development in psychometrics (Marinazzo et al., 2024). 

Outline - (3) Networks as Conceptual Framework. In section three ( Networks as Conceptual Framework ) it is pointed out that network psychometrics not only provides us with new statistical tools, but it is perhaps most productively framed as a general concep-tual rethinking of psychometric constructs. Interestingly enough, some basic psychometric concepts can be re-interpreted in a network context (Borsboom, 2023). In that light it seems important to keep in mind the distinction between statistical models and theories of psychological phenomena (van Bork et al., 2021; van Dongen et al., 2024). "network psychometrics". interim summary. We can roughly summarize by saying that "network models" isolate variance that is unique to variable pairs, while latent variable models focuses on variance that is shared across all variables. But they are closely related in that they both model a constrained covariance structure. Therefore, latent variable modeling and "network modeling" can be viewed from a purely statistical perspective much more productively as complementing each other instead of excluding or replacing the other (L. F. Bringmann & Eronen, 2018). For example, SEM usually requires us to impose more structure than we would perhaps want to for exploratory estimation, and the assumption of no latent variables in GGMs might not be so desirable as well. Initial exploratory factor analysis could there-fore be done by checking for clusters in a GGM to inform the number of factors (H. Golino et al., 2020; H. F. Golino & Epskamp, 2017). But it is important to keep in mind that the conventionally used estimation methods (Epskamp, Kruis, & Marsman, 2017; Isvoranu & Epskamp, 2023) try to estimate a sparse GGM (i.e., some elements in K constrained to be exact zeroes), which is only expected given a factor model when latent variables are orthogonal (Epskamp, Waldorp, et al., 2018). Moreover, in Christensen and Golino, 2021; Christensen et al., 2025 a network equivalent of factor loadings, which they call "network loadings" is proposed, and the applicability of SEM fit indices in Confirmatory Network Analysis (CNA; Du et al., 2025) has been evaluated.

first hint: conceptual possibilities. Marsman and Huth (2023) also point out that relations between these two types of network models could further our conceptual under-standing of psychometric constructs, which according to the above can be characterized by individual network topologies 30 . Therefore it might be insightful to establish any regular-ities in their structure, as it has been done in Network Science where several topological patterns like community structure or small-worldness are commonly observed in many dif-ferent applications (Newman, 2003). Are psychometric topologies similar or di ff erent and can we come up with plausible psychological processes that could generate them? 

second hint: Dynamic but also Complex. The last connection we will highlight might be helpful in addressing the issue of interpretability with increasing model complex-ity (number of parameters) and can perhaps also be viewed supplementary to the previous point and potentially help in enabling advances in research on some conceptual issues in psychology (van der Maas, 2024). In Catastrophe Theory 31 (Montaldi, 2021; Stewart & Peregoy, 1983), continuous and gradual changes in one or more control parameters of a complex system can lead to sudden and discontinuous changes in its dynamics. The Cusp is a specific type of catastrophe model with two control parameters, a bifurcation parame-ter (which can cause instability) and an asymmetry parameter (stable linear relationship), and has been applied in psychology to explain characteristics of depression (Cramer et al., 2016), visual perception (Ploeger et al., 2002) and attitude change (van der Maas et al., 2003). Cusp dynamics have also been associated with the Ising model in network psycho-metrics (Cramer et al., 2016; Finnemann et al., 2021; Loossens et al., 2020; van der Maas et al., 2017), and in the case of a fully connected IM (also called the Curie—Weiss model), with its mean-field behavior (van der Maas, 2024). Nevertheless, its practical use has been constrained by di ffi culties in performing model fitting and model comparison, to address this issue a modeling framework utilizing Mixture Structural Equation Models (MSEM) for cross-sectional data, or MSEM with regime-switching (MSEM-RS) for panel-data has been proposed in Chow et al. (2015).

An interesting discussion of dif-ferences in the application of networks in the natural and social sciences can be found in Hidalgo (2016). Although Random Graphs and Graph Theory in general can be seen as the initial basis for the more interdisciplinary e ff ort of Network Science, they represent fairly independent research fields which have focused on investigating di ff erent properties of networks (Iñiguez et al., 2020). Graph Theory. Graph Theory is a branch of mathematics that studies graphs, a mathematical structures for modeling (pairwise) relationships between objects. A graph 

G is typically defined as an ordered pair G = (V, E) where V is a set of vertices (or nodes /variables), and E ⊆ { (i, j) | i, j ∈ V} is a set of edges, which are pairs of vertices representing connections between them. A common distinction we have already encoun-tered previously is between directed graphs where E consists of ordered pairs of vertices, edges have a direction (( i, j) , ( j, i) → e.g., DAG /BN) and undirected graphs where E

consists of unordered pairs of vertices, edges have no direction (( u, v) = (v, u) → e.g., IM, GGM). Both can either have weighted edges, meaning each edge e ∈ E is associated with a weight or strength (e.g, partial-correlation coe ffi cient) or unweighted edges (e.g., ER model).

With regards to network psychometrics we can say that this almost exclusively—currently at least—refers to the estimation of some (covari-ance) matrix—representing pairwise variable interactions—with PGMs 53 from psychome-tric data, but as already mentioned some formal connections to Random Graphs already exist (Farasat et al., 2015; Marsman & Huth, 2023). Networks as Conceptual Framework 

As the above should have made clear, purely from the perspective of statistical mod-eling there is no clear boundary separating "network models" from "classical" psychometric latent variable models (e.g., Factor Analysis, SEM, IRT, GLM). Due to another interesting relation between IsingFit (the eLasso nodewise L1-regularized logistic-regression approach for estimating the IM (see Brusco et al., 2019)), Image Factor Analysis (IFA; Guttman, 1953) and IRT mentioned in Borsboom (2023) and Christensen and Golino (2021), some of the most central concepts in psychometric can be re-framed in a network context. Bors-boom (2023) proposed to conceptualize psychometric constructs as pragmatically selected sets of structurally connected 58 indicators (variables /items) of psychological phenomena. In that light Unidimensionality is approximated by a densely connected network, which as mentioned previously is the same as a unidimensional factor model (e.g., Curie-Weiss model ⇔ Rasch model (Marsman et al., 2018)) and can therefore be seen as a measure of network homogeneity. Therefore, mean-field approximations of PGMs (van der Maas, 2024) can be viewed as approximating a latent variable or the first factor in IFA (Bors-boom, 2023). For Validity the network perspective implies that psychometric constructs (e.g., psychopathologies, intelligence, personality) don’t refer to latent variables that cause item responses, but that the variables assessed through item responses are in fact part of a "construct network", and here psychometric construct can be viewed as a collections of 

structurally connected variables that interact with each other 59 . The observed behavior of this network we associate phenomenologically with subjective psychological states. Tak-ing this into consideration it seems useful to highlight the importance of di ff erentiating between empirical /methodological and conceptual /theoretical problems. 

Statistical Models, Theories, and Phenomena 

After the initial relations between statistical models in (network) psychometrics were established, the importance of di ff erentiating between statistical and conceptual equivalence has been stressed in the early application of network psychometrics in psy-chopathology research (L. F. Bringmann & Eronen, 2018) and in methodology develop-ment in general more recently (van Bork et al., 2021). The empirical literature of utilizing network psychometrics is nevertheless steadily growing (Robinaugh et al., 2020), but with-out any apparent cumulative theory building (e.g., in research on depression (Cai et al., 2024; Curtiss et al., 2022; Groen et al., 2019; Moron et al., 2024; Mullarkey et al., 2019; Ramm et al., 2025; Villacura-Herrera et al., 2025)). This has been identified as a fun-damental shortcoming of psychology, with one of the main contributing factors being the conflation of statistical and theoretical models due to the lack of strong formal theories (Fried, 2020; Kerschke, 2025). 

Formal Theories, Computational Models. However there is progress made in at-tempts to mitigate this issue, with a general discussion and outline of a methodology for productive explanations in psychology (van Dongen et al., 2024), awareness of how con-ceptual progress is essential and complementary in addressing empirical problems (Adolfi et al., 2024), as well as the development of general theory construction methodologies (Borsboom, van der Maas, et al., 2021; Haslbeck et al., 2022; Hawkins-Elder & Ward, 2020; Robinaugh et al., 2021). Computational models are essential in this research e ff ort (Guest & Martin, 2021), and a valuable tool for constructing formal theories when empiri-cal evidence is spares or indirect (Woensdregt et al., 2024). More concretely, some formal theories implemented as computational models have already been proposed in psychology (e.g., for emotion regulation (Chow et al., 2005), stress (Benthem de Grave et al., 2022), panic disorder (Robinaugh et al., 2024), suicidal ideation (L. Li & Li, 2024), cognitive behavioral therapy (Berwian et al., 2025; Norbury et al., 2024)), which can be utilized to better understand and advance treatment interventions (Mansell & Huddy, 2020; Ryan, Haslbeck, & Robinaugh, 2025) and dynamics in general (Cui, Wagenmakers, et al., 2025;

Dynamics, Graph Theory, and Complex Systems Idiographic Psychology. The vast majority of empirical research in network psy-chometrics analyze cross-sectional data (Punzi et al., 2022; Robinaugh et al., 2020; Schu-macher et al., 2024), but interpreting such finding as informative of within-person pro-cesses has been recognized as an ecological fallacy (Hamaker, 2025), the inability of group-to-individual generalizability is a serious issue (A. J. Fisher et al., 2018; Hunter et al., 2024; Speelman & McGann, 2020). The nature of ILD allows us—at least in principle—to di ff erentiate between-person (nomothetic) and within-person (idiographic) patterns (cross-sectional data = a mix of between- and within-person patterns), as well as learning about between-person di ff erences in within-person dynamics (Borsboom & Haslbeck, 2024; Hamaker, 2025). The first methodological bridge for di ff erentiating be-tween individual- and group-level patterns has of course already been introduced earlier with the Idiographic IM (Marsman & Huth, 2023). 

Dynamics. When trying to model psychological processes like individual develop-mental pathways the adoption of a complex dynamical systems perspective can be very ben-eficial (Hasselman, 2023), and has been invoked in psychometrics for re-conceptualizing depression (Cramer et al., 2016), psychopathology in general (Olthof, Hasselman, Oude Maatman, et al., 2023), change-processes in psychotherapy (Hayes & Andrews, 2020), substance use disorder (Witkiewitz & Tucker, 2025), transitions in mood (Waldorp & Kos-sakowski, 2020), phenomenology in psychopathology (Kyzar et al., 2025), as well as in educational psychology (Hilpert & Marchand, 2018). This also includes utilizing tool like 

Causal Loop Diagrams (CLDs 60 ; Barbrook-Johnson and Penn, 2022; Crielaard et al., 2024) and Perceived Causal Networks (PECAN; Burger et al., 2024; Scholten et al., 2025; Vogel et al., 2025) which can be combined with VAR models (Burger et al., 2022). 

Complex (Dynamical) Systems Theory. A clarifying discussion of what is meant by ’Complex (Dynamical) System’ can be found in Estrada (2024) and Mazzocchi (2025). For an elaboration of how this could be applied in psychology see van der Maas (2024). Patterns in ILD are likely complex, because EMA data is characterized by complex long-range temporal dependencies (Olthof, Hasselman, Wijnants, & Lichtwarck-Ascho ff , 2020; Olthof et al., 2024), and psychological self-ratings exhibit complex dynamics as well (Leis-ing et al., 2025; Olthof, Hasselman, Aas, et al., 2023; Olthof, Hasselman, & Lichtwarck-Ascho ff , 2020). Di ff erent metrics can be used for quantifying the complexity in time-series data, and George (2025) investigated how they relate to dynamics. Furthermore, this can be tied back very interestingly to statistical models with Coevolving Latent Space Networks with Attractors (CLSNA; Pan et al., 2025).

A particular EWS is Critical Slowing Down (CSD) which is an indi-cation of bifurcations (Evers et al., 2024), and has been applied in psychology to assess how this is related to changes in depression symptoms (Curtiss et al., 2023; Fried et al., 2023) and mood disorder during psychotherapy (Olthof, Hasselman, Strunk, et al., 2020). Specifically, in RNs the geometry of the phase space can function as a EWS (Hasselman, 2022). But more research is needed for the appropriate application of EWS in clinical practice (Cui, Olthof, et al., 2025), current modeling approaches are limited, and theoreti-cal foundations of psychological phenomena have to evolve with methods for data analysis in tandem here as well (Helmich et al., 2024). EWS are also researched in physiology (e.g., (Legault et al., 2024)), where networks in the context of complex dynamical systems are investigated as well (Schöll, 2022). 

Graph Theory. The goal should therefore be to apply mathematical formalisms that can adequately represent the relevant characteristics of psychometric constructs and in doing so connect psychological theory to empirical observations (Borsboom, 2023). Graph Theory represents an extensive researched formalism that can be applied to investigate properties of complex dynamical systems as networks (Porter & Gleeson, 2016), and could potentially be utilized more in psychometrics for doing that. But it is important to rec-ognize that this formalism is not assumption free either, but keeping this in mind, they can be chosen appropriately to match the applied context (Zamora-López & Gilson, 2024). Some vague suggestion for the uses of Graph Theory in the social and behavioral sciences have actually been mentioned some time ago already and include the potential for better quantifying structural and qualitative aspects of the constructs of interest (Harary & Nor-man, 1960; Tapiero, 1972). As previously mentioned, investigating the characteristics of graphs representing psychometrics constructs has been independently proposed again more recently in Marsman and Huth (2023). With some specific application being the use of a graph-theoretic similarity measure for comparing weighted adjacency matrices (Ulitzsch et al., 2023), and Perturbation Graphs for assessing experimental interventions and possibly discovering causal relations in psychology (Waldorp et al., 2025). Symmetry properties of graph limits and their implications for studying invariant sub-spaces in dynamical systems are investigated in Bick and Sclosa (2025), which could conceivably provide null-models for correlation networks (Masuda, Boyd, et al., 2025). A interesting discussion about learn-ing Graph structure from time-series data and the duality between predictability and recon-structability is given in C. Murphy et al. (2024) 61 . But this type of research is in its infancy and a more detailed and perhaps structured investigation of how graph-theoretic concepts could add to the psychometric-toolbox should probably be conducted in the future.

Conclusion Statistical Model Relations (Figure 1). The diagram of model relations in the form it is presented here has some limitations that could be addressed in the future. Even though it o ff ers an easier overview and entry point for more detailed explorations, accessing the necessary details for each model is still tedious. Furthermore, since the development of statistical methodology is continuously evolving, documenting available statistical models and their relations should ideally be organized in a form that allows for continuous updating and editing, ideally as an collaborative e ff ort (e.g., The Book of Statistical Proofs (Soch, 2025), or PsychoModels (van Dongen & Volz, 2024)). And perhaps also has some form of interactive interface for displaying model relations and includes "fact-sheets" which sum-marize essential information for each model (e.g., for univariate distributions in (Leemis & McQueston, 2008)). This could also include an e ff ort of collecting publicly available em-pirical datasets (e.g., https: // github.com /jmbh /EmotionTimeSeries) on which models could be compared. If tools and resources like this are available, access to it should be made as easy and convenient as possible to enhance empirical research. 

Methodology (Psychometrics) and Theory (Psychology). As we have tried to illustrate, the psychometric-toolbox has actually much more to o ff er then what is currently utilized and there are many more interesting paths forward. The development of statistical methodology in psychometrics could probably benefit from extending the connection to DST, the utility and perhaps need for doing so, we have tried to outline. We also hope to have drawn attention to the need for methodological developments in psychometrics being more explicitly and systematically linked to the processes of theory construction in psychology, for the benefit of both.
