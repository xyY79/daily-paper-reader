# LLMpedia: A Transparent Framework to Materialize an LLM’s Encyclopedic Knowledge at Scale

Abstract 

Benchmarks such as MMLU suggest flagship language models approach factuality satura-tion, with scores above 90%. We show this picture is incomplete. LLMpedia generates encyclopedic articles entirely from parametric memory, producing ∼1M articles across three model families without retrieval. For gpt-5-mini, the verifiable true rate on Wikipedia-covered subjects is only 74.7% – more than 15 percentage points below the benchmark-based picture, consistent with the availability bias of fixed-question evaluation. Beyond Wikipedia, frontier subjects verifiable only through cu-rated web evidence fall further to 63.2% true rate. Wikipedia covers just 61% of surfaced subjects, and three model families overlap by only 7.3% in subject choice. In a capture-trap benchmark inspired by prior analysis of Grokipedia, LLMpedia achieves substantially higher factuality at roughly half the textual similarity to Wikipedia. Unlike Grokipedia, every prompt, artifact, and evaluation ver-dict is publicly released, making LLMpedia the first fully open parametric encyclopedia – bridging factuality evaluation and knowledge materialization. All data, code, and a brows-able interface are at https://llmpedia. net .

1 Introduction 

Large language models are now used as everyday knowledge interfaces by hundreds of millions of people (OpenAI, 2025b). Yet what these mod-els actually know – and how reliably they express it in long-form text – remains poorly understood. Two research threads address this from opposite directions: factuality evaluation probes models on experimenter-chosen questions; knowledge mate-rialization lets models produce their own knowl-edge at scale. Neither alone suffices: benchmarks miss what they never ask, and materializations lack systematic evaluation. LLMpedia bridges the two. 

Figure 1: LLMpedia generates entirely from paramet-ric memory with full auditability. Grokipedia’s opaque pipeline shows evidence of retrieval-shaped genera-tion (Yasseri, 2025). 

Most current evaluation relies on curated bench-marks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022), and HLE (Phan et al., 2024). These are indispensable, but they share a structural limitation: they test only knowl-edge the evaluator thought to ask about. This creates a form of availability bias (Tversky and Kahneman, 1973): a model can look highly fac-tual on a fixed set of questions while contain-ing broad regions of weak or unstable knowledge the benchmark never reaches. The limitation be-comes sharper in real use, where people receive fluent, multi-paragraph explanations rather than short benchmark answers (Saeed et al., 2025a). A natural alternative is to materialize model knowledge at scale. Cohen et al. (2023) and GPTKB (Hu et al., 2025) showed that recur-sive elicitation can surface vast amounts of model knowledge, including large regions absent from Wikipedia (Hu et al., 2026; Ghosh et al., 2025). But GPTKB materializes triples – a format unlike how users actually interact with models (OpenAI, 2022) – and its pipeline suffers from persistent en-tity disambiguation failures (Ghosh et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2026). We instead materialize full en-

cyclopedic articles in the free-text modality users see, making it possible to evaluate subject choice, discourse structure, entity salience, and – crucially – how much apparent knowledge survives outside the narrow corridor of benchmark questions. This gap is large, for gpt-5-mini, the verifiable true rate on Wikipedia-covered subjects is only 74.7% – more than 15 percentage points below the 90%+ picture suggested by benchmark-style evaluation. That gap is the main empirical signal of this paper: fixed-question benchmarks overstate open-ended factual reliability in part because they are shaped by availability bias in what gets asked. Among surfaced subjects absent from Wikipedia but recoverable through curated web evidence, the true rate falls further to 63.2%. At the same time, Wikipedia covers only 61% of surfaced subjects, and three model families overlap on just 7.3%, in-dicating that materialized parametric knowledge is far more model-specific than leaderboard-style evaluation implies. This question matters beyond benchmarking. Wikipedia’s coverage is uneven, with a well-documented Western-centric bias (Wikipedia con-tributors, 2025b), gender skew (Wikipedia con-tributors, 2025a), and non-Anglophone gaps. If models encode reliable knowledge beyond Wikipedia, materializing it could help comple-ment those gaps – but only if the generation pro-cess is auditable. The most visible LLM-generated encyclopedia, Grokipedia (xAI, 2025), is method-ologically opaque. Prior work reports high tex-tual similarity to Wikipedia (Yasseri, 2025), and we document additional evidence consistent with retrieval-shaped generation (Appendix K). This creates a capture trap : the encyclopedia may in-herit Wikipedia’s phrasing while obscuring both genuine model knowledge and its error modes. We present LLMpedia , a fully auditable, open-source framework for parametric encyclopedia generation. Starting from a seed, the system expands breadth-first through article generation and wikilink extraction, then filters candidates through a three-stage sanitization pipeline before re-enqueuing them. Every prompt, intermedi-ate artifact, and evaluation verdict is publicly re-leased. We operate in two modes: general-domain materialization , where BFS expands freely from a seed entity, and topic-focused generation , where expansion is constrained to entities associated with one of three root topics. Both regimes use prompt templates documented in App. J.

Across gpt-5-mini (OpenAI, 2025a), Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024), and DeepSeek-V3 (Liu et al., 2024), we produce approximately one million articles. Motivated by claims of ideological bias in Grokipedia, we additionally inject controlled editorial per-sonas (scientific-neutral, left-leaning, conserva-tive) across all pipeline stages in topic-focused runs, treating framing as an explicit, auditable variable rather than a hidden confounder. Our investigation centers on three questions: 

RQ1 : What do topic-focused parametric encyclo-pedias reveal about cross-model factuality, cover-age, and the effect of editorial persona? 

RQ2 : At 10 5 scale, how do three model families differ in knowledge breadth, overlap, and reliabil-ity? Does factuality degrade with BFS depth? 

RQ3 : Does parametric generation escape the Wikipedia capture trap – producing factually com-petitive but structurally independent content? Our core contributions are: 1. A scalable, fully open-source pipeline for parametric encyclopedia construction –bridging factuality evaluation and knowledge materialization into a single framework; 2. Evidence that open-ended parametric genera-tion is substantially weaker than benchmark-style factuality suggests: for gpt-5-mini, true rate falls to 74.7% on Wikipedia-covered subjects, more than 15 points below the benchmark-based picture; 3. Evidence that the beyond-Wikipedia frontier is both real and difficult: subjects verifiable only through curated web evidence reach just 63.2% true rate; 4. A capture-trap benchmark, inspired by Yasseri (2025), in which we collect the most-edited English Wikipedia articles and show that LLMpedia achieves higher factuality than Grokipedia at roughly half the textual similarity to Wikipedia. 

2 Related Work 

Knowledge probing and its limits. Probing frameworks such as LAMA (Petroni et al., 2019) test recall via cloze prompts; multitask suites like MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) span knowledge-heavy domains; TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) targets misconceptions; HLE (Phan et al., 2025) pushes frontier difficulty. These measure only what the experimenter thought to ask. LLM-pedia complements benchmarks by materializing knowledge at scale and decomposing outputs into true, false, and unverifiable. 

From triples to articles. Cohen et al. (2023) introduced recursive elicitation via knowledge-graph expansion; Hu et al. (2025) scaled this into GPTKB (100M+ triples), revealing that many materialized entities are absent from Wikipedia (Ghosh et al., 2025). GPTKB material-izes triples; LLMpedia materializes full discourse-level articles. This is not merely a length differ-ence: articles make it possible to evaluate struc-ture, subject choice, and unverifiability in free text. 

Encyclopedia generation and the retrieval trap. 

Early work assumed structured inputs (Sauper and Barzilay, 2009; Lebret et al., 2016); WikiSum (Liu et al., 2018) scaled to multi-document summariza-tion. STORM (Shao et al., 2024) models pre-writing through web-grounded interactions but re-mains retrieval-dependent. Gao et al. (2024) found that outlines improve generation; LLMpedia ex-tends this with dynamic outline generation tai-lored to each entity. Grokipedia (xAI, 2025) oper-ates at the largest scale but discloses no method-ology; Yasseri (2025) report TF-IDF cosine of 0.46–0.49 against Wikipedia, nearly double LLM-pedia’s 0.26, suggesting retrieval-shaped repack-aging. 

Hallucination, retrieval, and provenance. 

Hallucination remains a central problem for factual generation (Ji et al., 2023). Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) can improve factuality by grounding outputs in external sources (Lewis et al., 2020; Mallen et al., 2023), while self-reflective variants and confidence elicitation can further improve reliability (He and Razniewski, 2025; Tian et al., 2023). But retrieval complicates provenance: once external passages are introduced, it becomes hard to sep-arate what comes from parametric memory from what is copied or assembled from retrieved text. In encyclopedia-style generation, this matters because retrieval can pull systems toward sources such as Wikipedia, obscuring whether the system is expressing model knowledge or producing retrieval-shaped content (Tuquero and PolitiFact, 2025; Yasseri, 2025). 

3 LLMpedia Framework 

Figure 2 shows the full pipeline. From a seed entity, the system expands breadth-first through optional self-grounding, outline generation, ar-ticle elicitation, and three-stage entity sanitiza-tion before enqueuing surviving children. All prompts and intermediate artifacts are logged. Full prompt templates for both general-domain and topic-focused runs appear in Appendix J. 

3.1 Materialization Modes General-domain expansion. In the general-domain setting, BFS starts from a single seed en-tity – Vannevar Bush in all reported runs – and expands without topical restrictions. Each arti-cle yields Wikitext with [[wikilinks]] ; after sanitization, surviving links become the next fron-tier. This mode surfaces an unrestricted knowl-edge graph and scales to 10 5–10 6 articles. 

Topic-focused generation. The topic-focused setting uses the same recursive BFS logic, but constrains expansion around one of three root topics: Ancient Babylon , US Civil Rights Move-ment , and Dutch Colonization in Southeast Asia .We seed each run with a topic-specific subject list and instruct the model to generate articles whose [[wikilinks]] remain directly asso-ciated with the root topic. Only topically rele-vant survivors are re-enqueued as children. This yields a controlled version of BFS useful for cross-model comparison, persona analysis, and domain-difficulty analysis. Prompts for both regimes are documented in Appendix J.

3.3 Dynamic Outline and Article Generation 

Prior work found that outlines improve LLM-based encyclopedia generation (Gao et al., 2024), but mainly studied fixed section templates. LLM-pedia instead uses dynamic outline generation: the model first proposes 6–7 section headings tailored to the entity type, then writes the article to match that outline. If warranted, the model also produces an {{ Infobox }} block. The model then generates Wikitext fol-lowing this outline: bolded title, concise lead, [[wikilinks]] , and optional 

[[Category:...]] tags. A typical arti-cle yields dozens of raw wikilink candidates, motivating the sanitization pipeline below.

3.5 Entity Sanitization Pipeline 

Both GPTKB (Hu et al., 2025) and GPTKB v1.5 (Hu et al., 2026) document persistent entity disambiguation and fragmentation failures. We address these with three stages; Figure 5 illustrates the effect on the general-domain expansion graph, where only encyclopedic entities survive to spawn sub-graphs. 

Stage 1: Canonical Normalization. Unicode NFKC, case-folding, and punctuation removal map surface variants to identical keys (e.g., “John F. Kennedy” → john f kennedy ). Fast and deterministic, this eliminates trivial duplicates be-fore costlier stages. 

Stage 2: LLM-Based Encyclopedic Filtering. 

Not every wikilink warrants its own article. LLMs exhibit a looping problem : expanding generic terms like [[engineer]] or [[inventor]] 

(Figure 4) yields thin articles that in turn produce equally generic wikilinks, diluting quality at each expansion layer. In contrast, 

[[Office of Scientific Research and Development]] expands into a rich, self-contained article with distinctive downstream entities. Traditional NER tools degrade on iso-lated entity labels; following GPTKB (Hu et al., 2025), we use the LLM itself to classify candidate batches (prompt in Appendix J), retaining entities that genuinely deserve standalone encyclopedia entries. 

Stage 3: Embedding-Based Deduplica-tion. Canonical normalization misses se-mantic duplicates (“Deutschland” vs. “Ger-many”). We maintain an embedding index (text-embedding-3-small ) over all com-mitted entities; candidates exceeding cosine threshold 0.90 are flagged and resolved via LLM arbitration with parent-article context. In parallel execution, concurrent NER workers may independently nominate the same candidate from different parents within the same BFS wave; within-wave cross-candidate deduplication at the similarity worker collapses these before any commit, guaranteeing the main queue contains no duplicates (Appendix E.2). 

3.6 Architecture and Persona Injection 

Each pipeline stage maintains an independent work queue with retries and exponential back-off, supporting both batch and online modes (Ap-pendix E.1). To study framing as an explicit vari-able, we inject three personas – scientific-neutral, left-leaning, and conservative – at the system level of all pipeline stages in topic-focused runs. This changes not only article prose, but also outline generation, link proposal, and filtering behavior.

5 Results 

5.1 Entity Sanitization at Scale 

The main scaling challenge is not article genera-tion alone, but sanitizing the much larger expan-sion frontier that generated articles create. GPT-5-mini produces articles for about 740K subjects, while Llama and DeepSeek produce articles for about 120K subjects each. Yet even these smaller open-model runs emit very large numbers of can-didate expansion links: 51.6M raw wikilink candi-dates for GPT-5-mini, 9.8M for Llama, and 6.5M for DeepSeek. Most of these candidates do not become new subjects. They are removed in three stages: lexi-cal/canonical deduplication, NER filtering, and se-mantic deduplication. After these stages, 741K candidates survive as new queued subjects for GPT-5-mini, compared with 498K for Llama and 396K for DeepSeek. These final counts are fron-tier nodes admitted into the BFS queue, not com-pleted articles. The dominant loss comes from redundancy. Relative to raw candidates, only 3.5% of GPT-5-mini candidates survive canonical deduplication, compared with 12.7% for Llama and 13.2% for DeepSeek. Among canonical survivors, NER ac-cepts 71.9% for GPT-5-mini, 54.8% for Llama, and 51.7% for DeepSeek; similarity filtering then retains 60.2%, 73.0%, and 89.3%, respectively. Overall, only 1.44% of GPT-5-mini raw candi-dates, 5.07% of Llama candidates, and 6.10% of DeepSeek candidates become new queued sub-jects. This gap reflects the fact that each article emits many wikilinks, many of which are dupli-cate surface forms, generic non-entities, or seman-tic near-duplicates.

5.3 RQ2: Cross-Model at 120K 

All three models start from the same seed (Vannevar Bush ) and expand under general-domain BFS. Because wikilink choices are model-dependent, the three graphs diverge rapidly after the first hop. We cap each corpus at 120K sub-jects and evaluate two 1K samples: one from the 

shared intersection and one from each model’s in-dependent corpus. Despite sharing a seed, only 7.3% of subjects appear in all three corpora (20,417 of 280,494 union). Early wikilink choices therefore cas-cade into substantially different subject graphs. On the shared intersection, GPT-5-mini leads at 94.2% precision, followed by DeepSeek (87.7%) and Llama (82.4%). On independent samples all models decline as coverage falls from 99.1% to roughly 81%. The ranking is stable across both conditions, indicating that the ordering is not merely an artifact of subject selection.

6 Conclusion 

Benchmarks report 90%+ factuality for frontier LLMs, but materialized writing reveals a weaker picture. On a uniform-random GPT-5-mini sam-ple, Wikipedia covers only 61.3% of subjects, with 74.7% true rate on the covered subset – over 15 points below benchmark performance. Web ver-ification confirms the gap: frontier precision is 79.8%. Three model families overlap on just 7.3% of subjects, and on 1,000 shared articles LLMpe-dia exceeds Grokipedia by +13 pp precision at half the textual similarity to Wikipedia. All data, code, and interface are at https://llmpedia.net .7 Limitations 

Single-run conditionality and cost barrier. 

Every article is the result of a single generation pass under a fixed prompt and temperature-0 de-coding. We do not claim that one pass fully char-acterizes what a model knows ; it surfaces one sam-ple from a latent knowledge distribution. Self-consistency decoding (Wang et al., 2022) could recover additional knowledge, but the full project already costs approximately $3,000 across gener-ation, ablation, evaluation, and preliminary testing (Appendix B); multi-sample exploration would multiply the dominant generation cost consider-ably. As shown in (Giordano and Razniewski, 2025), the choice of the seed entity is inconse-quential. 

Temporal snapshot. All runs were conducted between January and March 2026 with the target models listed in 4. Wikipedia and web references were retrieved during the evaluation window, but both evolve continuously. A claim judged unver-ifiable today may become verifiable or refutable later. 

Evaluation stack and judge reliability. Auto-matic LLM-based factuality evaluation is practical at our scale but not perfect. Our pipeline relies on a single judge model, gpt-4.1-nano , for both claim extraction and verdict assignment, but this is common practice in research (Hu et al., 2025; Saeed et al., 2025b). 

Web evidence coverage and selection bias. For frontier subjects, only 75.7% (292/386) yielded usable web evidence under the strict source-quality filtering described in Appendix D. The re-maining 24.3% are excluded from Tier 2 metrics, which may bias frontier estimates toward subjects with stronger public documentation. 

Scale asymmetry. Only gpt-5-mini reaches nearly one million articles. The open-weight mod-els are constrained to approximately 120K–498K, so the deepest hop-stratified analysis is GPT-5-mini specific. 

Scope of measurement. Our framework cap-tures propositional factual support, but not every-thing that matters in an encyclopedia article. It does not directly measure coherence, neutrality, salience, completeness, or omission of important facts. An article can therefore achieve high pre-cision while still being editorially unbalanced or incomplete. 

Model and data-source limitations. Although our goal is to analyze encyclopedic generation rather than surface or publish personal data, gen-erated articles and public-source evidence may oc-casionally mention real individuals because ency-clopedic subjects often include public figures. We did not design the pipeline to expose private or sensitive personal information, and any such con-tent that is found inadvertently should be filtered from releases and removed from public-facing in-terfaces or downloadable artifacts.

C Capture-Trap Benchmark: Full Details 

C.1 Experimental Design 

The capture-trap benchmark asks whether para-metric generation can remain structurally inde-pendent on subjects where a retrieval-shaped sys-tem has maximal opportunity to track Wikipedia closely. 

Title selection. Inspired by Yasseri (2025), we collected 1,000 of the most-edited En-glish Wikipedia titles that were also present on Grokipedia. Generation. LLMpedia generated all 1,000 ar-ticles using gpt-5-mini in the baseline large-scale configuration (no self-grounding, minimal reasoning). Grokipedia articles were fetched from the live site. Wikipedia references were retrieved through the MediaWiki API with redirect resolu-tion. 

Evaluation. For each title, we extracted up to 10 atomic claims from both systems and verified them against two evidence tiers: Tier 1 

(Wikipedia) and Tier 2 (curated web sources). Similarity metrics were computed against Wikipedia only. This is the same evaluation pipeline used in the main paper.

Model configuration and stage independence. 

Each pipeline stage—self-grounding, outline gen-eration, article elicitation, NER filtering, and simi-larity arbitration—can be assigned an independent language model, temperature, and token budget. In practice, all stages within a given run share a single model unless explicitly differentiated. The one principled exception is the embedding model used for similarity deduplication, which is al-ways configured independently: embedding and completion models serve fundamentally different functions and conflating their configuration would silently degrade deduplication quality. 

BFS depth and article budget. Expansion depth and total article count are both configurable hard caps. In topic-focused runs (§5.2), depth is capped at two hops from the thematic seed to maintain topical coherence. In general-domain expansion (§5.3), no depth cap is imposed: GPT-5-mini reaches hop 6 over 741K articles, with 94.2% of the corpus at hop 4 or beyond (Ap-pendix I). In both modes, the seed article at hop 0 is generated but excluded from evaluation metrics to avoid confounding the model’s generation be-haviour with the trivially known anchor entity. 

Similarity threshold and arbitration. The co-sine similarity threshold above which two entity embeddings trigger a deduplication check is set to 0.90. This value is conservative: surface-form variants of the same entity (e.g. United States and 

USA ) consistently fall above it, while genuinely distinct but thematically related entities consis-tently fall below it. When the threshold is ex-ceeded, a secondary LLM arbitration step is in-voked rather than rejecting automatically. This two-stage design prevents both false positives and false negatives. 

NER confidence and elicitation filtering. Un-der the calibrated prompt variants, both the NER stage and the elicitation stage assign explicit con-fidence scores. Candidates below the NER con-fidence threshold are rejected before reaching the similarity stage. Rejection at either stage is non-permanent: if a future parent article re-proposes the same entity with sufficient confidence, it re-enters the pipeline from the beginning. 

Persona. Each pipeline stage accepts a persona specification injected at the system level. Three personas are provided: scientifically neutral, left-leaning, and conservative. All three share identical structural prompts; only the framing and evalua-tive language differ. Fault tolerance and strict-gate semantics. 

Failed API calls are retried with exponential back-off up to a configurable maximum. After all retries are exhausted, a strict-gate policy applies: a stage that cannot produce a valid output causes the cor-responding entity to be dropped rather than passed through with a degraded result. For NER, a parse failure produces no candidates rather than pass-ing all candidates unchecked. For similarity ar-bitration, an API failure causes the candidate to be treated as a duplicate and rejected. This conserva-tive policy introduces a small downward bias on recall but eliminates the risk of contaminating the corpus with candidates that bypassed quality con-trols. 

E.2 Deduplication Correctness Under Parallel Execution 

LLMPedia maintains four independent parallel queues: an elicitation queue , a NER queue , a 

similarity queue , and the canon queue . Workers on each queue operate concurrently and indepen-dently. This parallelism creates a structural race: multiple NER workers can extract and accept the same entity from different parent articles within the same BFS wave, before any of them reaches the similarity worker. Figure 6 shows a concrete instance and its resolution. 

The main article queue is structurally duplicate-free. CANON QUEUE is the only 

channel through which an entity can reach article generation. An entity enters CANON QUEUE 

if and only if it simultaneously passes Stage 3 similarity and is absent from C ANON KEYS ; both operations execute atomically. 

Why commitment happens after similarity, not after NER. A candidate is registered in CANON KEYS only after it passes Stage 3. If com-mitment happened at NER acceptance, an entity later rejected by similarity would be permanently suppressed even though it was never materialised. Deferring commitment keeps future parent articles free to re-propose the same entity. 

Within-batch collisions. A single parent article may produce the same wikilink in two surface forms that canonicalize identically. Within-wave cross-candidate deduplication handles this before any index update.

regime performs open BFS expansion from an arbitrary seed with no topical constraint. The 

topic-focused regime also performs BFS but restricts expansion to entities with a direct, meaningful connection to a designated root topic (e.g., Ancient Babylon ). Orthogonally,

Runtime placeholders. All prompts share the following runtime-filled placeholders: • {subject name }: the entity currently be-ing processed, e.g. Vannevar Bush .• {root subject }: the root topic constrain-ing topic-focused runs, e.g. Ancient Babylon ;absent in general-domain prompts. • {avg words per article }: target arti-cle length in words (default 716, matching Wikipedia’s overall average (Wikipedia con-tributors, 2026)). • {outline block }: the JSON section list produced by the outline generation step and injected verbatim into elicitation prompts; see Example J below. • {phrases block }: the newline-separated list of raw wikilink candidates extracted from the generated article, passed to NER filtering; see Example J below. • {persona block }: one of three fixed in-struction strings injected at the system level of every pipeline stage—elicitation, NER, and similarity arbitration—to probe how ed-itorial stance shapes content selection and framing independently of subject matter; see Example J below. 

Example 1: {outline block }. The outline step calls the model once per subject and produces a subject-tailored JSON section list. The output is injected verbatim into the elicitation prompt; section titles are used exactly as == Heading == markers and the model is forbidden from adding, removing, or reordering them. This en-forces structural consistency across models and experimental conditions while allowing content that adapts to the subject type—a historical fig-ure yields biography-oriented sections; a chemical compound or a city would yield an entirely differ-ent structure.

left leaning :

All three persona variants share identical structural prompts; only the framing and evaluative register differ. K Grokipedia Insights 

Grokipedia’s construction process is not publicly disclosed. Through examination of published articles, we identified indicators of retrieval-augmented rather than parametric generation. 

Internal tool-calling traces. Figure 16 shows an article where internal tool-calling dialogue is visible in published output—diagnostic of a pipeline where the generation model calls external retrieval tools at inference time. 

Entity disambiguation failures. Figures 17–19 show three classes of disambiguation failure: apolitician conflated with a basketball player (Fig-ure 17), conflicting biographical facts from differ-ent individuals (Figure 18), and three individuals blended into one article (Figure 19). These errors are characteristic of retrieval over a corpus where homonymous entities co-occur. 

Consistency with capture-trap results. These qualitative observations align with the quantitative capture-trap findings (§5.4): Grokipedia’s high Wikipedia similarity (TF-IDF 0.492) combined with low factual precision (76.7%) and high false rate (20.3%) is consistent with a retrieval pipeline that tracks Wikipedia’s surface form but intro-duces errors through entity disambiguation and passage conflation.
