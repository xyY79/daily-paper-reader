Abstract 

The explosive growth of AI research has created unprecedented information overload, increasing the demand for scientific sum-marization at multiple levels of granularity beyond traditional ab-stracts. While LLMs are increasingly adopted for summarization, existing benchmarks remain limited in scale, target only a single granularity, and predate the LLM era. Moreover, since the release of ChatGPT in November 2022, researchers have rapidly adopted LLMs for drafting manuscripts themselves, fundamentally trans-forming scientific writing, yet no resource exists to analyze how this writing has evolved. To bridge these gaps, we introduce SciZoom , a benchmark comprising 44,946 papers from four top-tier ML venues (NeurIPS, ICLR, ICML, EMNLP) spanning 2020 to 2025, explicitly stratified into Pre-LLM and Post-LLM eras. SciZoom provides three hierarchical summarization targets (Abstract, Contributions, and TL;DR) achieving compression ratios up to 600:1, enabling both multi-granularity summarization research and temporal mining of scientific writing patterns. Our linguistic analysis reveals strik-ing shifts in phrase patterns (up to 10 × for formulaic expressions) and rhetorical style (23% decline in hedging), suggesting that LLM-assisted writing produces more confident yet homogenized prose. 

SciZoom serves as both a challenging benchmark and a unique resource for mining the evolution of scientific discourse in the generative AI era. Our code and dataset are publicly available on GitHub and Hugging Face, respectively.

1 Introduction 

The landscape of scientific research is witnessing an explosive ex-pansion, particularly within Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Natural Language Processing (NLP), a trend extensively documented in the science of science literature [ 16 ]. The volume of scientific litera-ture has grown exponentially, with indexed articles increasing by 47% between 2016 and 2022 alone [ 17 ], leaving researchers to face unprecedented information overload. To mitigate this challenge, the research community has traditionally relied on abstracts as the primary medium for summarization, fostering numerous datasets and modeling approaches [12, 28]. However, relying solely on abstracts presents significant limita-tions. Abstracts themselves often remain dense and technical, de-manding significant cognitive effort to parse effectively. Researchers require more efficient ways to assess relevance before committing to full-text reading. To address this, two additional elements have emerged as critical: the articulation of specific contributions, es-sential for understanding a paper’s core novelty [ 37 ], and extreme summaries such as TL;DR (Too Long; Didn’t Read), now a required field in venues such as top-tier AI conferences [ 11 ]. Together, these form a natural hierarchy of scientific simplification, from full text to abstract to contributions to TL;DR. The release of Large Language Models (LLMs) [ 10 ] has accelerated this trend, with researchers increasingly leveraging them to generate and refine summaries at various granularities. Yet LLMs are not limited to summarization. Since the release of ChatGPT in November 2022, researchers have rapidly adopted these models for drafting, editing, and polishing full manuscripts themselves [ 22 ]. This widespread adoption raises a critical question for the data mining community: How have the linguistic patterns of scientific writing evolved across the LLM era? While Liang et al. [ 22 ] identified vocabulary shifts in peer reviews, no study has systematically mined how paper writing itself has changed across multiple granularities. Existing benchmarks such as SciTLDR [ 11 ]predate this era and cannot support such temporal analysis. 1 Introduction 

The landscape of scientific research is witnessing an explosive ex-pansion, particularly within Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Natural Language Processing (NLP). The number of submissions to top-tier conferences has grown dramatically, with NeurIPS alone receiv-ing over 21,000 submissions in 2025, more than double the 9,454 in 2020 [ 2], leaving researchers to face an unprecedented level of information overload. To mitigate this challenge, the research com-munity has traditionally relied on abstracts as the primary medium for summarization, fostering numerous datasets and modeling ap-proaches [4, 8]. However, relying solely on abstracts presents significant limi-tations. Abstracts themselves often remain dense, technical, and lengthy, demanding significant cognitive effort to parse effectively. Essentially, an abstract functions as a miniature version of the paper, which does not fully resolve the need for rapid screening. Researchers require more efficient ways to assess the relevance of a paper before committing to full-text reading. Beyond the abstract, two key elements have emerged as critical for scientific communication. First, authors prioritize the articu-lation of their specific contributions during the publication pro-cess. Identifying these core novelties is essential for understanding the intrinsic value of a research paper [ 9]. Second, the need for rapid screening has popularized extreme summaries such as the TL;DR (Too Long; Didn’t Read), now a required field in venues like ICLR [3]. Despite the importance of these elements, there is a notable ab-sence of a unified benchmark. Existing datasets such as SciTLDR [ 3 ]pioneered the extreme summarization task but are limited by their small scale and reliance on peer-review comments. Moreover, pre-vious benchmarks typically lack the structured aspect of key con-tributions, focusing only on single-level summarization. Most critically, existing datasets are rooted in the era before 2020 and fail to capture the profound impact of Large Language Models (LLMs) on scientific writing styles. While recent studies have quantified surface-level shifts in vocabulary and frequency [ 5 ], they rarely address the deeper representational divergence between human-crafted and AI-assisted texts. To truly understand how LLMs reshape scientific discourse, we must analyze changes not just in word usage, but in the semantic latent space itself. However, there is currently no large-scale resource that supports such comparative representational analysis across the pre- and post-LLM eras. 2 Related Work 

Scientific Document Summarization. The task of summarizing sci-entific documents has evolved considerably. Early work focused on abstract generation, with discourse-aware attention proposed for long documents [ 12 ]. Subsequent studies introduced transformer-based approaches [ 32 ] and efficient attention mechanisms for han-dling lengthy scientific texts [ 18 ], while critical evaluations high-lighted persistent challenges in faithfulness and factual consis-tency [ 20 ]. Structured summarization with predefined facets such as purpose, method, and findings [ 28 ] later emerged. The demand for rapid screening led to neural extreme summarization: SciTLDR [ 11 ]introduced single-sentence summaries for 3,229 papers from a sin-gle venue, ICLR, relying on reviewer-written rather than author-provided TL;DRs. CiteSum [ 27 ] scaled to 93K papers using citation sentences, ScisummNet [ 40 ] incorporated citation networks for content-impact modeling, and multimodal fusion for TL;DR gener-ation has also been explored [ 5 ]. Despite these advances, existing benchmarks target single granularity levels and lack the hierarchi-cal structure needed for multi-level simplification research. 

Knowledge Mining from Scientific Text. Extracting structured knowledge from papers is central to scientific data mining. Argu-mentative Zoning [ 37 ] pioneered sentence classification by rhetori-cal roles such as background or method. The SemEval-2021 NLP-ContributionGraph task [ 14 ] formalized contribution extraction as structured prediction, and recent work has analyzed contribu-tion patterns across 29K NLP papers [ 33 ]. At the corpus level, S2ORC [ 25 ] released 81 million papers with full text, OAG-Bench [ 41 ]introduced 10 academic graph mining tasks, and Microsoft Aca-demic Graph [ 35 ] enabled web-scale knowledge exploration, while explosive growth in AI conference submissions has been docu-mented [ 6 ]. LLM Impact on Scientific Writing. The release of ChatGPT in November 2022, building on advances in instruction-tuned mod-els [ 30 ] and open-source alternatives [ 38 ], prompted systematic investigation into its impact on academic communication. Analysis of peer reviews from major machine learning conferences identi-fied linguistic markers of LLM-assisted writing: adjectives such as “commendable,” “meticulous,” and “intricate” increased 10–35 × in frequency, with an estimated 6.5%–16.9% of reviews containing sub-stantial LLM-generated content [ 22 ]. A follow-up study extending this analysis to over one million papers found that approximately 22.5% of computer science paper abstracts were substantially mod-ified by LLMs by September 2024, compared with lower rates in mathematics (7.7%) and the Nature portfolio (8.9%) [23]. Despite these important findings, existing studies focus primarily on vocabulary-level features and do not examine deeper patterns such as phrase structures or hedging behavior. Moreover, no study has investigated how LLM influence varies across different gran-ularities of scientific text. Abstract Collection. Abstracts are directly available as structured metadata in OpenReview submissions, ensuring 100% coverage across the corpus without additional processing. 

Contribution Extraction Pipeline. Unlike abstracts and TL;DRs, key contributions are not provided as structured metadata in Open-Review. Authors typically articulate contributions at the end of the introduction using explicit markers and bullet points. To extract these systematically, we employ a three-stage cascading pipeline as illustrated in Figure 2. First, rule-based extraction identifies ex-plicit contribution markers, e.g., “Our contributions are:” or “We make the following contributions,” and extracts the subsequent bulleted items. Second, LLM filtering uses Qwen3-4B-Instruct [ 39 ]to verify whether the extracted items constitute valid research contributions. Third, for papers where extraction fails or valida-tion is rejected, generative fallback synthesizes contributions from the abstract and introduction. The first two stages successfully ex-tracted author-written contributions from 15,397 papers, covering 34% of the corpus, while the remaining 29,549 papers required LLM generation. 

TL;DR Collection. TL;DRs are author-provided fields in Open-Review submissions. As shown in Table 3, 21,295 papers include TL;DRs, with higher availability in recent years due to venue policy changes.

4 Experiments 4.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate zero-shot LLMs on three summarization tasks: Abstract (𝐺 2), Contribution ( 𝐺 3), and TL;DR ( 𝐺 4) generation from full paper text ( 𝐺 1). 

Evaluation Metrics. Summarization evaluation requires captur-ing both surface-level fidelity and deeper semantic alignment, as no single metric adequately reflects summary quality [ 15 ]. We there-fore adopt a complementary suite spanning lexical, semantic, and embedding-based measures. For lexical overlap, we report ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L [ 24 ], the de facto standard for summarization benchmarks, mea-suring unigram recall, bigram precision, and longest common sub-sequence respectively. We additionally report BLEU-4 [31], which evaluates precision-oriented n-gram matching and is particularly in-formative for short-form outputs such as TL;DRs where every word choice matters. METEOR [ 8] complements these by incorporat-ing synonym matching and stemming, providing a recall-oriented perspective that better handles paraphrastic variation common in scientific writing. However, lexical metrics are known to underestimate quality when generated text conveys the same meaning through different wording [ 42 ]. To address this, we include BERTScore [ 42 ], which computes token-level cosine similarity over contextual embeddings SciZoom: A Large-scale Benchmark for Hierarchical Scientific Summarization across the LLM Era KDD ’26, August 2026, Jeju, South Korea 

following the sentence embedding paradigm [ 34 ], capturing se-mantic equivalence beyond surface forms. This embedding-based evaluation is particularly important for our hierarchical setting, where the same core contribution must be faithfully preserved from abstract (177 words) down to TL;DR (20 words) despite radical lexical divergence. 

4.2 Baselines 

We benchmark six open-source LLMs in zero-shot settings, pairing older and newer versions within each model family to analyze cross-era generalization. The Mistral family includes v0.1 with a knowledge cutoff of late 2022 and v0.3 with a cutoff of late 2023 [ 19 ]. For Llama, we compare Llama-3-8B-Instruct with Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct [ 13 ]. The Qwen family pairs Qwen-7B [ 7] with Qwen2-7B-Instruct [ 36 ]. This design enables analysis of whether newer model releases exhibit improved robustness to the linguistic shifts observed in Post-LLM papers. The Post-LLM models have knowledge cutoffs that overlap with our Post-LLM test set, meaning some papers may have been seen during pretraining. This potential data contamination makes our benchmark a conservative evaluation, as models may perform better on Post-LLM papers due to memorization rather than generalization. All models receive the full paper text as input and generate outputs for each granularity level independently using greedy decoding with a maximum output length of 512 tokens for abstracts and contributions, and 64 tokens for TL;DRs. For embedding-based analysis, we employ NV-Embed-v2 [ 21 ], a state-of-the-art model on the MTEB benchmark [ 29 ], to measure se-mantic similarity between generated and ground-truth summaries (Table 10), conduct cross-granularity retrieval experiments (Tables 8 and 9), and visualize hierarchical summary embeddings via UMAP (Figure 4). We select three representative models—Mistral-7B-v0.1, Llama-3-8B, and Qwen2-7B—for these analyses based on their sum-marization performance.

4.5 Era-based Analysis 

The explicit Pre-LLM and Post-LLM stratification in SciZoom en-ables analysis of how model performance varies across eras. 

Cross-Era Generalization. We evaluated all models separately on 

Pre-LLM and Post-LLM test sets to assess generalization across eras. 

Post-LLM models show stable or improved performance: Qwen2-7B gains +2.3 ROUGE-1 points on contribution extraction when evalu-ated on Post-LLM papers, while Llama-3.1-8B remains stable across eras. In contrast, older Pre-LLM models exhibit notable degrada-tion: Mistral-v0.1 drops 3.6 points and Qwen-7B drops 4.1 points on abstract generation when evaluated on Post-LLM papers. This asym-metry suggests that newer models have adapted to post-ChatGPT writing patterns.

Stable Contribution Patterns. Contrary to expectations of “contri-bution inflation,” the number and length of claimed contributions remain remarkably stable across eras, with 4.3 contributions aver-aging 113 words in Pre-LLM versus 4.2 contributions averaging 111 words in Post-LLM . TL;DR Adoption Growth. The TL;DR availability rate increased from 40.4% in Pre-LLM to 51.6% in Post-LLM , reflecting the commu-nity’s growing recognition of extreme summarization’s value for rapid paper screening. This trend is partly driven by venue policy changes, with ICLR making TL;DR a required field in recent years. 

5 Discussion 

Implications for Scientific Communication. Our findings reveal measurable homogenization in scientific prose following LLM adop-tion. The 4–10 × increase in formulaic phrases, combined with a 22.8% decline in hedging language while assertive language remains stable, suggests that LLM assistance systematically strips epistemic caution rather than adding confidence, raising questions about peer review calibration and AI disclosure policies. 

Limitations. SciZoom covers four AI/ML venues; linguistic pat-terns may differ in other domains. Our contribution extraction pipeline relies on LLM generation for 66% of papers, potentially introducing stylistic artifacts, and TL;DR availability is limited to 47.4% due to venue policy variations. Our temporal stratification also uses ChatGPT’s release as a binary threshold, though LLM adoption likely followed a gradual trajectory. 

Future Directions. SciZoom enables fine-tuning for multi-granularity summarization, cross-domain analysis of linguistic shifts, and devel-oping era-aware models that account for Pre/Post-LLM distribution shifts. 

6 Conclusion 

We introduced SciZoom , a large-scale hierarchical benchmark de-signed to overcome the critical limitations of scale and temporal coverage in existing scientific summarization datasets. By curating 44,946 papers from four premier AI conferences, we established an annotation schema mapping full texts to structured contributions and extreme summaries. Beyond its utility as a benchmarking re-source, SciZoom serves as a digital archive capturing the paradigm shift between Pre-LLM and Post-LLM eras. We empirically demon-strated that this distribution shift necessitates robust, era-aware models, and we envision SciZoom as a foundational asset for de-veloping next-generation AI assistants capable of navigating the expanding ocean of scientific knowledge. We release SciZoom pub-licly to facilitate future shared tasks and community benchmarking efforts.

B Contribution Extraction Pipeline 

Algorithm 1 formalizes our three-stage contribution extraction pipeline. Stage 1 applies regex patterns to identify explicit contribu-tion markers (e.g., “Our contributions are:”) and extracts subsequent bullet points. Stage 2 uses Qwen3-4B-Instruct to validate whether extracted items constitute genuine research contributions, filter-ing false positives from related work or methodology descriptions. Stage 3 serves as a generative fallback for papers lacking explicit contribution sections, synthesizing contributions from the abstract and introduction.
