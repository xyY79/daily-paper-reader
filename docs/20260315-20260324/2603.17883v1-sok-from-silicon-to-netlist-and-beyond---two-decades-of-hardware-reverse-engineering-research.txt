## Abstract 

As hardware serves as the root of trust in modern computing systems, Hardware Reverse Engineering (HRE) is founda-tional for security assurance. In practice, HRE enables critical security applications, including design verification, supply-chain assurance, and vulnerability discovery. Over the past two decades, academic research on Integrated Circuit (IC), Field-Programmable Gate Array (FPGA), and netlist reverse engineering has steadily grown. However, knowledge remains fragmented across domains and communities, which compli-cates assessing the state of the art and hampers identifying shared research challenges. In this paper, we present a system-atization of knowledge based on an in-depth analysis of 187 peer-reviewed publications. Using this corpus, we character-ize technical methods across the HRE workflow and identify technical and organizational challenges that impede research progress. We analyze all 30 artifacts from our corpus using established artifact evaluation practices. Key results could be reproduced for only seven publications (4%). Based on our findings, we derive stakeholder-centric recommendations for academia, industry, and government to enable more coordi-nated and reproducible HRE research. These recommenda-tions target three cross-cutting opportunities: (i) improving reproducibility and reuse via artifact-centric practices, (ii) en-abling rigorous comparability through standardized bench-marks and evaluation metrics, and (iii) improving legal clarity for public HRE research. 

## 1 Introduction 

Modern computing systems increasingly rely on a silicon root of trust, particularly in high-assurance and safety-critical domains [32]. However, hardware manufacturing is character-ized by globally distributed and complex supply chains, which introduce opportunities for the insertion of malicious func-tionality such as hardware Trojans [2], counterfeiting [64], and intellectual property theft [89]. Recognition of these sup-ply chain vulnerabilities catalyzed research into Hardware Reverse Engineering ( HRE )—the process of reconstructing higher levels of abstraction from fabricated hardware or in-termediate design representations [27]—as an essential ca-pability for establishing and verifying trust in the hardware supply chain [178]. In practice, HRE supports critical security tasks including vulnerability discovery [133], design verifi-cation through hardware Trojan detection [140], and counter-feit avoidance [5, 12]. As HRE can also be used offensively to extract design secrets [88] or facilitate hardware-level at-tacks [80, 90, 93], public HRE research is equally essential for assessing attacker capabilities and evaluating protections. 1985 1990 1995 2000 2005 2010 2015 2020 2025

RQ1: What technical approaches exist for HRE? 

To answer RQ1, we conducted an extensive literature review and compiled a systematization of techniques for IC , FPGA ,and netlist reverse engineering across 187 peer-reviewed pub-lications. In the process, we observed recurring issues that appear to hinder progress across the field, motivating our second research question: 

RQ2: What are the technical and organizational challenges hindering the advancement of HRE? In addressing RQ2, we analyzed these challenges and ob-served substantial overlap across HRE subdomains. In par-ticular, published techniques are only sporadically compared, evaluation metrics and benchmarks vary across publications, and prior results are rarely used as direct foundations for subsequent work. In search of a root cause, we define our final research question, which focuses on the availability and quality of artifacts such as implementations and benchmarks: 

RQ3: To what extent do HRE publications provide artifacts that are available, functional, and reproducible? In response to RQ3, we find that the scarcity of high-quality artifacts appears to contribute to limited reproducibility and, consequently, comparability across publications. Correspondingly, we propose concrete stakeholder-centric actions for academia, industry, and government that target (i) promoting reproducibility and reusability, (ii) community-driven efforts for comparability, and (iii) strengthening the legal framework surrounding public HRE research. We argue that acting systematically and jointly on these opportunities is a necessary step toward evolving HRE into a more coordinated research discipline. 

## 2 Methodology 

To provide a holistic overview of peer-reviewed HRE research, we consider the complete end-to-end process, spanning from gate-level netlist recovery from IC s or FPGA s to subsequent netlist reverse engineering. As illustrated in Figure 2, we followed a structured four-stage analysis pipeline, guided by the methodology of Wolfswinkel et al. [192]. Starting with database searches (Step A), we filter and organize the discovered papers (Step B). The retained papers are then used as a seed set to iteratively expand the corpus through citation mining, with newly identified papers subjected to the same filtering process (Step B). We then analyze the resulting papers in detail (Step C) and examine the available artifacts according to the common artifact badge categories 

Availability , Functionality , and Reproducibility (Step D). 

## 2.1 Analysis Pipeline 

Step A: Literature Search. We identified 571 papers through database searches of DBLP, Semantic Scholar, and Google Scholar using a search query (see Appendix A) that was designed based on domain expertise and highly cited HRE publications [12, 56, 199]. To ensure comprehensive coverage of HRE literature, we performed backward and for-ward citation mining using Crossref and Semantic Scholar. Backward mining identified works cited within our corpus, while forward mining identified publications that referenced papers in the corpus. A paper was included if it appeared at least three times in backward or forward mining, balancing coverage and manageability by filtering weakly connected works. This step added 402 publications over four iterations, bringing the total count to 973 papers. The final iteration in December 2025 added only one paper, indicating saturation. 

Step B: Select & Organize Relevant Papers. In each it-eration, we filtered papers for relevance in two stages. First, to determine whether a paper contributed to HRE , one au-thor screened each title and abstract and proposed an initial decision, which was then discussed and finalized with two additional authors. After the final iteration, this process had resulted in 344 publications for subsequent categorization. Second, two authors reviewed each paper and labeled it ac-cording to the topics it covers, following the codebook in Table 2. For each paper, exactly one primary code and any number of secondary codes were assigned. Additionally, each paper received at least one type label from the codebook in Table 3 based on its primary contribution. Disagreements were resolved with a third author to reach a final decision. In both stages, papers were excluded if they were not in English, not peer-reviewed, behind paywalls that prevented access despite standard institutional subscriptions, or written as technical reports, dissertations, or tutorials. Papers were also excluded if they did not primarily contribute to HRE research. Examples for such exclusions include research on pedagogical aspects, hardware design, Trojans, obfuscation, or side-channel analysis. Ultimately, this process yielded a final corpus of 187 papers, divided into three categories based on their primary topic: 87 focused on IC , 23 on FPGA , and 77 on netlist reverse engineering. 

Step C: Distill Techniques & Challenges. To answer RQ1 and RQ2, three teams of two researchers—one team per ma-jor topic (i. e., IC , FPGA , and netlist reverse engineering)— analyzed all publications. Step D: Artifact Evaluation. We categorized all HRE ar-tifacts as tools, images, or other resources (e. g., scripts or Register Transfer Level ( RTL ) code). We then evaluated all artifacts with respect to Availability , Functionality , and Re-producibility . For availability, we distinguish between perma-nent repositories (e. g., Zenodo), non-permanent hosting (e. g., GitHub or personal websites), availability upon request, and unavailable artifacts (no longer hosted or no response to re-quests). Functionality comprises completeness (verifying that all claimed components are provided), documentation quality ,and exercisability (successful execution), with exercisability assessed only for artifacts classified as tools. The evaluation was conducted retrospectively, without artifact au-thor interaction. Two undergraduate research assistants with prior HRE experience performed the hands-on evaluation and documented their findings for each artifact using a structured template. Senior researchers supervised the process through regular review and discussion meetings. On average, each artifact required approximately six hours of evaluation effort. 

## 2.2 Limitations 

Our literature search may have excluded relevant work in other languages. Additionally, our exclusion criteria may have removed papers containing relevant insights at the boundaries of our defined scope. These methodological trade-offs were necessary to maintain feasibility, and we argue their impact was mitigated through iterative backward and forward citation mining until saturation. Furthermore, parts of the screening and in-depth analysis relied on individual assessments, and— as with any interpretive review—alternative coding or inclu-sion decisions could lead to slightly different classifications. To reduce such effects, all selection, labeling, and analysis decisions were systematically reviewed through multi-author discussions and consensus procedures. 

## 3 Techniques and Challenges (RQ1 & RQ2) 

HRE comprises two major phases: netlist recovery and netlist analysis [6]. Netlist recovery differs significantly between IC s and FPGA s [179]. Figure 3 provides an overview of the individual steps of IC , FPGA , and netlist reverse engineering, synthesizing the technical approaches identified across the literature and highlighting their interdependencies. Hence, this section is organized into three parts, each addressing RQ1 (techniques reported in the literature) and RQ2 (challenges hindering the advancement of reverse engineering). 

## 3.1 From IC to Netlist 

Modern digital IC s consist of large-scale Boolean logic circuits, realized by a few hundred thousand to many bil-lions [65] of nanoscopic transistors on the polysilicon layer. Stacked on top, multiple layers of metal routing connect those transistors to form a circuit. Extracting the netlist entails recovering transistors and interconnection layers alike, gener-ally in a destructive manner, relying extensively on advanced imaging and computer vision techniques.

I1 Sample Preparation. An IC is protected by an en-capsulation, also known as a package. The package ensures physical durability [11] and provides connections, e. g., to a circuit board [63]. This package must be removed to access the die before the delayering process can reveal the circuit. 

Depackaging. Methods for depackaging depend on the package material. Packages are typically made of plastic, metal, or ceramic [11, 114]. Plastic packages constitute the majority and are primarily removed using acid [87, 113, 149]. The literature provides information on the ratios of acid mix-tures and processing temperatures required for effective re-moval [80, 114]. Some packages allow for the top cover to be removed with simple mechanical means instead [112]. Advanced packages may encapsulate multiple dies under a single metal lid, in which case the metal can be removed by milling [135]. Decapsulation of ceramic packages has not been discussed in our corpus of HRE literature. 

Cross-Section. A first step in analyzing a die is often sacri-ficing one sample for cross-sectioning by cutting across all of its layers. Examining the cross-section allows identifying the materials and thickness of each layer [99, 103, 135], which then can guide the choice of delayering methods. 

Delayering. Layer-removal methodologies can be classi-fied into several categories. Wet etching removes material through chemical reactions in liquid solutions [87, 103, 112]. 

Mechanical etching relies on direct mechanical contact with a solid tool or abrasive [80, 102, 103, 133, 134, 149]. It may be combined with chemistry, as in chemical-mechanical pol-ishing [135]. Dry etching comprises a variety of techniques in the gas phase or vacuum using ions, plasma, or reactive gases [87, 103]. For instance, in ion milling , a beam of ener-getic ions is accelerated toward the sample, physically sput-tering atoms from its surface [113, 147, 148]. Ion milling can be chemically assisted [177]; however, material removal remains ion-driven. It is not to be confused with reactive ion etching , which also uses ion bombardment but removes material primarily chemically [85, 138]. During the delayering process, it is important to keep the surface planar [9, 35, 80, 150], and to know when to stop to avoid damage to subsequent layers, a process called end-point detection. The endpoint can be detected using manual Scanning Electron Microscope ( SEM ) inspection [103], or other methods such as ultraviolet photon spectroscopy emis-sion [43], or optical microscopy [102, 134, 149]. 

I2 Imaging. Imaging IC s is usually done with an SEM .Proper use requires balancing parameters to obtain reliable results in reasonable time, and improper settings can produce low-quality images. Optimal settings vary depending on the instrument, even when imaging the same layer of the same IC [107]. Settings and detector choice also depend on the type of layer to be imaged. While detectors can be com-bined [101], they are typically used individually. For metal layers, backscattered electron detectors are preferred, as they provide the necessary contrast to distinguish metal from sur-rounding insulation [87, 148]. For imaging transistor gates, however, secondary electron detectors are used to reveal fine surface topography [10, 11]. 

I3 Stitching & Stacking. Since the field of view of an SEM is limited, it cannot capture an entire layer at once. Consequently, each layer is scanned in a two-dimensional grid, with every image depicting only a small tile of the layer. To reconstruct transitions between images, tiles need to be stitched together. To facilitate this stitching, im-ages are taken with an overlap. Although some modern imaging systems offer automated stitching, researchers of-ten use custom algorithms [173] or adapt tools from other domains [80, 133]. To this end, adjacent tiles are first posi-tioned locally, i. e., relative to each other, leveraging overlap using techniques such as phase correlation [98, 153], normal-ized cross-correlation [153], or feature extraction [103, 153]. Because local stitching can be imperfect, global stitching 4optimizes the mosaic by considering multiple neighboring tiles, either sequentially [16] or simultaneously [102,153]. To recover signal routing across layers and reveal the IC ’s 3D structure, the stitched layers must be stacked . To this end, an-chor points are defined between adjacent layers, and the layer image is warped accordingly [143]. Notably, we found no use of deep learning for stitching, though it has been applied to stack alignment [98, 153] and to detect stitching errors [98]. 

I4 Semantic Segmentation. Wires, vias, transistor gates, and active regions are identified through semantic segmenta-tion, with optional pre- and post-processing. 

Pre-Processing. IC images can be pre-processed to fa-cilitate subsequent recognition methods, although these ap-proaches do not always improve results [108]. This step typically has two objectives, which are often achieved using classical computer vision methods: First, noise is suppressed through averaging of multiple images [103], or spatial filters incorporating surrounding pixels [108, 117]. Second, adjust-ing brightness and contrast corrects for visual inconsisten-cies between tiles caused by acquisition variations [108, 171]. Deep learning has also been applied for noise reduction [195], but requires retraining for each new dataset [62]. 

Segmentation. To eventually reconstruct a gate-level netlist, all visible circuit elements on each layer must be identified. Historically, this identification was performed manually by crawling over printed images [173], and later using digital an-notation software [80, 144]. However, with the rapid increase in transistor counts, manual analysis has become impractical, and thus automated methods have emerged. For metal layers, researchers first relied on classical computer vision methods that group pixels based on intensity thresholds [11, 117, 118] and texture [189], or detect boundaries through brightness gradients [101]. More sophisticated techniques take design rules, such as track thickness or spacing, into account [101]. An established classical approach for detecting standard cells on the polysilicon and active layer is template matching. Here, image regions are compared against a library of visual reference patterns [36, 144]. If details about the manufactur-ing process are unknown, a standard cell library might first need to be extracted from the images, either manually [133] or automatically through finding common patterns [104, 209]. Other research adopts techniques used for metal track segmen-tation to automatically detect standard cells by individually identifying transistors and their interconnections [74, 143]. Recently, the focus has gradually shifted to machine-learning approaches [70]. The plethora of such research utilizes supervised learning [26, 97, 103, 104, 176, 197] on all IC layers; however, these models require massive amounts of labeled training data. Manually labeling real IC images is time-consuming [23, 144]. To bridge this gap, standard data augmentation—such as random rotations and intensity shifts— is used to artificially expand limited datasets [167, 203]. Be-yond simple augmentation, researchers have investigated gen-erating synthetic images from layout masks, i. e., Graphic Data System II ( GDSII ) files. These provide a ground truth and enable training at scale [94,113,190,194]. Despite promis-ing results with synthetic data [26], domain transfer of a trained model from one IC to another is limited, as IC s vary significantly in appearance. Consequently, we observe a push toward unsupervised learning techniques [147] or automatic pseudo-label generation [24]. Recent publications propose using transfer learning. To this end, Meta’s SAM has been fine-tuned on IC images [132]. Another technique relies on Convolutional Neural Networks ( CNN s) pretrained on the generic ImageNet dataset to extract general visual features from IC images without fine-tuning [171]. Another interest-ing approach, in cases when no ground truth is available, is to transform other images with existing ground truth to resemble the unlabeled images. A model trained on these images can then be applied to the unlabeled data [26, 168]. 

Post-Processing. Post-processing can be applied to iden-tify or reduce segmentation errors. This prevents misclas-sifications from propagating into semantic violations in the extracted netlist, such as unintended open or short circuits. Approaches from the literature correct segmentations based on neighboring pixel values, also resulting in smoother bound-aries and less noise [26, 46, 203]. Other post-processing pro-cedures check for design rule violations related to, e. g., via size [117], wire width [102], or spacing [117]. Another ap-proach detects segmentation errors using CNNs [207]. 

I5 Netlist Extraction. After all images are stitched, stacked, and segmented, a gate-level netlist can be extracted, abstracting the physical layout into a network of Boolean logic gates and sequential elements. The connection be-tween logic gates can be reconstructed by tracing vias and wires [74,103,133]. The extraction mechanism for logic gates is dependent on the employed segmentation technique. Where standard cells have been identified using template matching, the connection of each cell to the metal layers above can be immediately identified [103]. If transistor-level segmentation was used, the logic function realized by the transistors in each standard cell must first be identified [47, 74, 143]. To this end, subgraph matching is commonly used [74, 141].

IC reverse engineering challenges are primarily driven by advancements in semiconductor manufacturing [12, 173].

Error Propagation. The success of extracting a netlist from an IC depends on image recognition algorithms and on the quality of images themselves [12, 70, 107]. Con-tamination such as dust particles can obscure critical circuit features [35, 138] and makes even manual annotation diffi-cult [169]. During delayering, non-uniform milling or etching can leave residue, causing brightness variations and geomet-ric distortions [101, 113] or, conversely, fully erode parts of the layer [134]. Furthermore, suboptimal sample preparation affects the quality of SEM imaging. For instance, residue can exacerbate charge-up effects [70,101,134]. During imaging it-self, poor parameter choice, e. g., for acceleration voltage and dwell time, can deteriorate image quality [101, 116]. Finally, most semantic segmentation algorithms assume error-free in-puts, which is impossible to achieve in practice [12]. Certain circuit structures, combined with poor image quality, also pose challenges. Thin, closely spaced, or rough metal lines are difficult to distinguish in images, which leads to classifica-tion errors [169]. Due to the interdependence between steps and the risk of irreversible errors [143], achieving perfect results requires multiple samples of the same IC [12]. 

Human Factors and Resource Limitations. Many stages in IC reverse engineering rely on manual intervention [12], and the success of sample preparation and imaging depends strongly on operator skills. For instance, delayering must stop at a precise depth to avoid obscuring features or destroying the sample [134, 149]. Similarly, SEM imaging quality is sensitive to manual parameter choices [100]. However, re-liance on human intuition inherently introduces inter-operator variability [149, 190]. This puts the field in a quandary where manual intervention remains both irreplaceable and undesir-able. To mitigate this issue, researchers have explored tools for individual sub-steps, such as automated layer-thickness estimation [149]. However, fully automating the IC reverse engineering process appears unlikely in the near future. Advanced equipment can improve both throughput and quality [107]. However, modern tools are often prohibitively expensive and not universally available [12, 99]. Beyond equipment, access to specialized facilities, e. g., cleanrooms, is needed for advanced technology nodes. Consequently, re-verse engineering state-of-the-art IC s requires experienced op-erators and well-equipped laboratories, creating a significant entry barrier [12]. The difficulty to obtain IC images, together with the scarcity of open datasets [190], led researchers to use synthetic images for developing recognition algorithms. However, the effects of using synthetic IC images are under-studied and require further investigation. 

Limited Generalizability of Segmentation Models. Even for ideal images, recognition methods struggle to generalize across technology nodes and IC layers [190]. This domain shift problem [25,168] stems from two primary sources: First, the shapes and materials used in IC s vary significantly across nodes [191]. Second, sample preparation and imaging cause variations in resolution, magnification, brightness, contrast, and noise [102, 108, 189, 190]. Obtaining a sufficiently di-verse dataset is impractical [168, 196], hampering the de-velopment of more robust methods. These generalization challenges necessitate continuous parameter tuning or retrain-ing, affecting both classical computer vision and machine learning [23, 98, 102, 108]. While researchers are exploring solutions such as transfer learning [98, 170], automated IC recognition across diverse conditions remains unsolved. 

Image Analysis Validation. When designing recognition algorithms, validation depends on accurate ground-truth la-bels and appropriate metrics, raising two concerns. First, man-ual image labeling is an arduous and costly task [188, 190], particularly for large IC s [22], and prone to human error [176]. Publications relying instead on synthetically generated SEM images currently do not consistently disclose this practice. Second, comparisons of an algorithm’s output with a ground truth often use pixel-accuracy metrics such as Intersection over Union ( IoU ) [70,191], which do not fully reflect semantic accuracy [143]. Domain-adapted metrics such as Electrically Significant Differences ( ESD ) have been proposed [108], but are not widely adopted. Still, manual corrections remain necessary [203], since error-free automated recognition is un-realistic to achieve [102, 143]. While improved image quality 6may reduce the amount of manual intervention, it must be weighed against the cost of longer imaging times [191]. 

## 3.2 From FPGA to Netlist 

FPGA s are reconfigurable hardware devices that can be pro-grammed to implement almost any digital circuit. To achieve this, a bitstream file in a proprietary format is loaded into the FPGA ’s configuration memory, which is typically im-plemented using Static Random Access Memory ( SRAM ). Because this memory is volatile, the FPGA must be re-programmed on every power-up. Hence, the bitstream must be stored in an external non-volatile memory. To recover a gate-level netlist from an FPGA bitstream, one must under-stand its proprietary file format, recover the bitstream, poten-tially decrypt it, and finally convert it into a netlist [179]. 

3.2.1 Techniques for FPGA Reverse Engineering (RQ1) 

Based on our literature review, we split FPGA reverse engi-neering into four major steps: F1 Bitstream Format Reverse Engineering, F2 Bitstream Extraction, F3 Bitstream De-cryption, and finally F4 Bitstream Conversion. The majority of our analysis is grounded in the 23 FPGA papers included in our final corpus. However, because the number of FPGA reverse engineering publications is relatively small, we also extracted relevant information from work that was initially excluded—e. g., concerning bitstream protections and bit-stream manipulation. 

F1 Bitstream Format Reverse Engineering. Bitstreams follow a standard binary file structure, comprising metadata, control commands, cryptographic material, checksums, and the configuration data that defines the functionality of the pro-grammable FPGA fabric. To reverse-engineer a bitstream’s high-level structure, prior work has mostly relied on public documentation from FPGA vendors [130, 202, 210]. Un-documented structure information can be reverse-engineered through manual analysis using Electronic Design Automa-tion ( EDA ) tool features such as debug bitstreams [81], or by reverse-engineering vendor software [126, 164]. The configuration data encodes both the functionality of logic elements, aka. Programmable Logic Points ( PLP s), such as Lookup tables ( LUT s), Flip Flops ( FF s), or hard Intellectual Property ( IP ) blocks like DSPs, BRAMs, and PCIe, as well as the routing between them via Programmable Interconnect Points ( PIP s) [44]. Vendors typically do not disclose the mapping from configuration bits in the bitstream to the corresponding physical components in the FPGA fab-ric [163]. To reveal this proprietary relationship, researchers often construct hardware designs using vendor-provided EDA tools, generate corresponding bitstreams, and then systemat-ically modify the positions and functions of individual pro-grammable elements. By comparing bitstream differences before and after each change, they identify which bits config-ure specific logic or routing elements [8, 86, 206]. While this methodology remains consistent across studies, nuanced vari-ations were introduced to improve accuracy and performance. Table 1 provides an overview of the different approaches. Initially, a correlation-based approach was proposed, in which designs are synthesized, and the behavior of individ-ual bits across multiple bitstreams is correlated with the re-sources used by each design [8]. This approach was later improved by generating bitstreams that contain isolated logic or routing elements, enabling a direct one-to-one mapping between each configurable element and the bits that control it [29]. To reduce computational effort, some studies have leveraged the regular structure of many FPGA architectures rather than explicitly mapping every configuration bit [8,206]. Because similar elements often share a consistent configura-tion pattern in the bitstream, reverse engineering such patterns once can suffice to extrapolate them to other instances across the device [202]. The format reverse engineering process may be further sped up by reducing the number of generated bitstreams—either by partitioning bitstreams to extract infor-mation about multiple elements simultaneously [205], or by combining information from different bitstreams [29]. 

F2 Bitstream Extraction. Most papers treat bitstream extraction as a technical challenge and either tap the memory buses connecting the non-volatile memory to the FPGA [129, 202] or dump the memory [56]. Some research mentions niche methods in which the bitstream is loaded by a co-processor, allowing for extraction via software analysis [88] or reading back the bitstream directly from the FPGA via (hidden) debug mechanisms [156, 210]. Overall, bitstream extraction receives little attention in the academic literature. 

F3 Bitstream Decryption. Most modern FPGA s provide encryption and authentication to protect bitstreams. How-ever, prior work has shown that these bitstream protections can often be broken or circumvented by (i) side channel at-tacks based on leaked information through power or electro-magnetic radiation [68, 126, 164], (ii) optical attacks leaking encryption keys [106, 165], or (iii) attacks exploiting bugs in the implementation of security mechanisms [49 –51]. We deem bitstream protection mechanisms out of scope for our study and focus on the reverse engineering of unencrypted bitstreams, as most works on FPGA reverse engineering do. 

F4 Bitstream Conversion. In the literature, the process of converting a bitstream into a gate-level netlist comprises two steps: (i) the recovery of logic elements and (ii) the reconstruction of routing. The behavior of logic elements can be inferred from the corresponding configuration bits in the reversed bit mapping. 3.2.2 Challenges in FPGA Reverse Engineering (RQ2) 

Based on our literature review, we identify five open chal-lenges in FPGA reverse engineering. 

(Verified) Completeness. Modern FPGA s are highly com-plex and include device-specific features beyond the classical configurable elements, e. g., hard IP blocks such as DSPs and PCIe interfaces. A major technical challenge is achieving and verifying the completeness of the recovered bitstream format in a black-box setting. To solve this challenge, additional information, such as default values applied at power-up and a comprehensive understanding of the configuration process itself, is required [187]. Although some studies claim perfect recovery of the entire bitstream format, they validate their work only on minimal benchmarks (see Table 1). 

Reliance on Deprecated Tooling and Narrow Focus. 

Many existing approaches rely on the ability to control each configurable element in isolation [28, 205, 206]. While older EDA tools such as AMD 1 ISE, which has been deprecated since 2013, often allowed for such fine-grained control, it is no longer feasible in modern tools such as AMD Vivado [205]. Although some progress was made adapting legacy reverse-engineering workflows to more recent toolchains, the afore-mentioned issues have not been overcome, especially for routing [30]. Consequently, many proposed methods can-not be applied to current-generation devices, including the speed-ups proposed by Zhang et al. [205]. Existing research on bitstream reverse engineering exhibits a disproportionate focus on AMD FPGA s, see Table 1. The resulting over-representation of a single vendor could stem from the lack of public low-level documentation from other vendors. The absence of other manufacturers and the lack of generalization leave a significant gap in research. 

Scattered Information. FPGA bitstream documentation is used not only for reverse engineering but also for de-sign applications. Therefore, relevant knowledge is scat-tered across research communities, which hampers a com-prehensive understanding. Knowledge is distributed among hardware reverse engineers, open-source hardware design-ers [33, 40, 54, 172, 175], specialized groups focusing on bit-

Limitations in Methodology and Validation. Overall, re-search on FPGA reverse engineering is of mixed quality. Of-ten, works omit crucial details, rendering independent re-production or use of their techniques challenging, even for domain experts, further worsened by the fact that only two publications release reference implementations. Evaluations often validate only a subset of the stated claims, limiting confidence in the reported results and obscuring the rigor of the underlying methods. Moreover, some publications offer limited novelty by revisiting problems already solved in prior work. These issues impede cumulative progress and may explain the lack of FPGA reverse engineering publications at top-tier venues. This scarcity may also be supported by ven-dors’ efforts to protect their IP through licensing restrictions that try to legally prohibit bitstream analysis. Collectively, these limitations may explain the lack of publicly available, peer-reviewed academic tools capable of recovering arbitrary netlists from FPGA bitstreams. For now, open-source initia-tives such as Project X-Ray [54] and IceStorm [33], while not achieving 100 % completeness, represent the only available and usable solutions to this end. 

## 3.3 Netlist Reverse Engineering 

Both I5 Netlist Extraction and F3 Bitstream Conversion yield a gate-level netlist consisting of combinational gates (e. g., AND, OR, and XOR gates), sequential logic elements (e. g., FF s and latches), and their interconnections. Such a netlist is often described as a “sea of gates” [3], reflecting the scale and structural complexity of the extracted design. Netlist reverse engineering aims to interpret this sea of gates by recovering higher-level information and reintroducing ab-stractions removed during design and manufacturing. Its goals range from identifying regions of interest (e. g., for lo-cating fault-injection targets) [93] to fully understanding an implementation, including its algorithms [88], e. g., to verify the absence of backdoors in third-party designs [122]. 

3.3.1 Techniques for Netlist Reverse Engineering (RQ1) 

Netlist reverse engineering can be broadly organized into four steps: N1 Partitioning, N2 Module Identification, 

N3 Algorithmic Recovery, and finally N4 Sensemaking. 

N1 Partitioning. Commonly, the netlist is partitioned by assigning gates to groups that correspond to functional cores, structures such as registers and arithmetic subcircuits, or just categories such as datapath and control path [161]. 

Cores. Several works suggest that connectivity is denser within a module than across module boundaries. To this end, Cakir et al. cluster gates using eigenvalue decompositions of the adjacency matrix [18, 19], Weber et al. apply graph clustering algorithms to gate-level netlists [182], and Couch et al. use an approximation of the NCut criterion [34]. Werner et al. employ graph clustering, refined with structural properties, and combined with visual netlist graph inspection [184]. Another cue for separating cores is physical proximity: due to routing and timing constraints, related cells are often placed in close proximity. Such placement information can be used, for example, as input to a Graph Neural Network ( GNN ) that partitions the netlist into clusters [72]. 

Word-Level Structures. Partitioning can also focus on smaller structures, grouping gates of the same word-level register or (arithmetic) operation. Physical proximity helps cluster single-bit elements into words [131]. Many methods assume that each bit of a datapath word undergoes the same computation, so their surrounding logic should be structurally similar. Similarity can be measured by comparing fan-in cuts [122, 166], computing graph edit distances over local neighborhoods [131], or using BERT-style models [204]. To better separate words with similar fan-in structure, Meade et al. incorporate additional structural features and apply Prin-cipal Component Analysis ( PCA ) to embed bits of the same word nearby in a high-dimensional space [122]. A complementary heuristic groups gates by shared control signals: since those steered by the same control typically be-long to the same word [3, 27, 57, 166]. Albartus et al. extend this approach by potentially incorporating expected register sizes and examining shared predecessor and successor groups, based on the insight that all bits of a word-level register typi-cally connect to the same surrounding registers and belong to the same register stage [3]. Subsequent work refines this by using known word groupings from other analyses [88], and leveraging FPGA primitives that already expose word-level interfaces [119]. The shared successor and predecessor assumption can also be used to propagate known word bound-aries, e. g., by symbolically evaluating the combinational logic around an already identified word [95, 161, 162]. 

Control Logic. Many approaches partition circuits into datapath and control path by identifying Finite State Ma-chine ( FSM ) FF s that belong to the control path. Here, the datapath-similarity assumption is inverted: FF s whose fan-in structure is dissimilar to word-level datapath logic are likely control logic. This can be done by structural comparison of fan-in cuts [14, 120] or by comparing the same cuts at a functional level [60]. Another heuristic assumes control logic connects to many parts of the circuit. Chowdury et al. use this assumption and calculate graph centrality metrics as inputs to a GNN that classifies FF s as control or datapath [31]. A spe-cialized approach targeting block ciphers analyzes the circuit under induced FF faults, assuming faults in control logic pro-duce different effects than those in datapath registers [125]. 

N2 Module Identification. Next, functionality is assigned either to modules obtained from partitioning or by using an 9interplay of partitioning and module identification. 

Datapath. Most commonly, datapath functionality is iden-tified by exploiting structural similarities between similar high-level modules within the implemented circuit. This en-ables the creation of libraries comprising known templates, which are then matched against unknown (parts of) netlists. The central challenge is thus measuring similarity between candidate subcircuits and reference implementations. Early work explored subgraph isomorphism on subcircuits or whole designs [13,27], but its computational cost motivates alternative comparisons. These include handcrafted subgraph embeddings combined with classical machine learning [7], graph-similarity measures [57] and embeddings [34], and hyper-dimensional graph encoding [61]. Other approaches compute node embeddings from local neighborhoods and sample them for machine-learning-based classification [55] or match nodes individually against a pattern library [7, 37, 155]. Some techniques use GNN s to learn node embeddings and predict the operation a node is part of, trained on labeled pattern libraries [4, 17, 67, 139, 181, 208]. Beyond structural matching, extracted cuts can also be compared functionally to find semantically equivalent matches, independent of their structural implementation [95, 151, 161, 162]. Not all methods rely on generic matching. Several ap-proaches search for domain-specific characteristics, such as FF structures for Linear Feedback Shift Registers ( LFSR s) or counters [161, 162, 180], or carry chains hinting at arith-metic operations [88,128]. On architectures without dedicated carry primitives, half adders can be reconstructed from XOR patterns [160, 183]. Related techniques reintroduce (half-)adder primitives to ease equivalence checking, using func-tional bitslice analysis [110], rewriting via e-graphs [198], or GNN s [193]. Similarly, Leander et al. exploit the structure of block ciphers to detect cryptographic implementations [93]. Another issue is reconstructing the order of subcircuit In-put/Output ( I/O ) signals and mapping them to operands or templates. For arithmetic subcircuits, this I/O mapping can be deduced from structural or functional patterns. A common cue is that the least significant input bits affect many output bits, whereas the most significant bits influence fewer out-puts [59,67,69,88,181]. Other approaches evaluate the circuit on all-zero or one-hot/cold inputs [27, 45, 151]. Some tech-niques even use such behavioral signatures to perform module identification and I/O mapping simultaneously [96, 158]. Given a functional hypothesis for an unknown subcircuit, formal verification is commonly used to confirm equivalence. Depending on the setting, this includes Boolean Satisfiabil-ity ( SAT ) and Satisfiability Modulo Theories ( SMT ) solv-ing [42, 59, 88, 183, 201], Quantified Boolean Formula ( QBF )solving [59, 95, 128, 161], Binary Decision Diagram ( BDD )matching [27, 45], Symbolic Computer Algebra ( SCA )-based techniques [110, 111, 198], polynomial rewriting [69, 200], linear coefficient fitting [69], and symbolic regression [69]. 

Control Logic. Existing work also aims to identify FF s that form an FSM state register. Such methods often exploit the interconnections of state bits: since the next state is com-puted based on the current one, state registers often form cyclic dependencies. Accordingly, such approaches search for Strongly Connected Components ( SCC s) or feedback paths [15, 27, 83, 84, 137, 152, 179]. State registers can also be identified by analyzing whether FF fan-outs connect to control nets such as enable, reset, or MUX select [88, 127, 152]. 

N3 Algorithmic Recovery. A netlist lifted to higher-level modules can be described as a “sea of modules”, reflecting the introduced abstraction and the reduction in complexity [161]. However, to later understand the implemented computations, the challenge is to recover an algorithmic description from these modules. For algorithmic recovery, the reverse engi-neer is often faced with data-dependent sequential behavior. They need to combine recovered control-path and datapath information to compile a more explicit representation. 

FSM State Graph. For control logic, algorithmic recovery often resorts to reconstructing FSM state graphs. Given the FSM subcircuit, these methods analyze state transitions to determine reachable states and their transitions, typically ex-porting the result as a state traversal graph. This can be done by brute-force enumeration of reachable states [179] or by analyzing next-state logic using Automatic Test Pattern Gen-eration ( ATPG ) [83], Reduced Ordered Binary Decision Dia-gram ( ROBDD ), or SAT -based reasoning [121,123,124,137]. 

Control and Datapath Combination. Integrating results from multiple reverse-engineering steps remains difficult and is rarely addressed explicitly. Narayanan et al. adapt existing techniques and combine them with novel methods to extract an RTL description [128]. Klix et al. present a solution toward achieving a higher-level algorithm description comprising two components: (i) manual tracing of simulation results obtained from real-world inputs, and (ii) an automated hybrid of concrete and symbolic simulation that outputs a Python replication of the circuit behavior [88]. 

N4 Sensemaking. Sensemaking is the final step after suc-cessive abstraction recovery: interpreting the recovered algo-rithm in its operational context. This stage is largely unex-plored; so far, prior work has only proposed manual analysis by domain experts [88]. 

3.3.2 Challenges in Netlist Reverse Engineering (RQ2) 

Based on our literature review, we identify four key challenges in current netlist reverse engineering research. 

Focus on Early Stages. Existing work often targets early-stage tasks, particularly partitioning and module identifi-cation [6]. These stages decompose a netlist into (identi-fied) word-level modules and possibly even larger functional 10 blocks. However, many important applications, such as vul-nerability discovery, require insights beyond the word level. A holistic design understanding is only achieved through al-gorithmic recovery and higher-level sensemaking. In turn, these processes require interpreting datapath and control logic in conjunction, often by introducing additional abstractions. These later stages remain largely unexplored, with a few ex-ceptions suggesting that dynamic analysis is a promising research direction [88, 146]. We argue that efforts to improve its scalability, together with developing complementary tools, could advance netlist reverse engineering. 

Hurdles for Automation. Manual netlist reverse engineer-ing is highly time-consuming, making automation essential for practical and economic viability [57, 93]. While many recent works propose algorithms for individual subproblems, end-to-end automation remains elusive. Isolated solutions are often difficult to combine: they may be non-interoperable, rely on incompatible assumptions (e. g., specific architectures or design layouts), or require idealized input from preced-ing steps. Further, many approaches depend on hand-tuned parameters or thresholds, fundamentally limiting full automa-tion [6,14,31,83,122,131]. Effective integration also requires standardized interfaces and data formats for exchanging in-termediate results, and ideally a common framework. While such frameworks exist [157, 179], adoption is limited. Even with a unified toolchain, human experts must interpret and val-idate intermediate results [27, 88, 161], highlighting the need for tools that efficiently integrate analyst expertise and for further study of how analysts perform these tasks [56, 185]. 

Overly Idealized Assumptions. Many proposed algo-rithms rely on assumptions that rarely hold in practice. Al-most all assume error-free netlists as input [7, 27, 57, 71, 142, 184], which is unrealistic given the error-prone netlist recov-ery process for IC s. Some module identification approaches further assume perfect partitioning into (yet unidentified) mod-ules [161] or a known I/O mapping from library candidates to such unidentified modules [27, 59, 66, 82], both of which are challenging and error-prone tasks on their own. Other algorithms are evaluated either on unoptimized netlists—e. g., without logic merging across module boundaries [4]—or on task-specific netlists that only include arithmetic components, sidestepping cases that break their assumptions and can trig-ger false detections or excessive runtimes [69, 105]. Several control-logic identification methods assume that FSMs form a small, separable SCC , yet real designs often collapse con-trol and datapath into a single large SCC due to feedback loops [88]. Together, these assumptions make it difficult to assess algorithm effectiveness under realistic conditions. 

Barriers to Cumulative Progress. Several core chal-lenges in netlist reverse engineering—some noted as early as 1999 [27]—remain central in recent work [88], and many solution strategies show substantial overlap. While individual techniques have evolved, overall progress appears limited. During our research, we rarely observed techniques build-ing upon one another, or even just quantitatively comparing themselves to previous work. Hence, we assume that the observed stagnation in netlist reverse engineering progress is largely driven by poor reusability and comparability of existing work. Moreover, we found that methodological de-scriptions are often insufficient to reproduce results or validate claims, preventing cumulative and incremental research and forcing authors to repeatedly re-address known problems. The field also lacks widely adopted tools [88] and standard-ized benchmarks [88, 121], making fair comparison across approaches difficult and obscuring genuine progress. Several factors contribute to this situation. Legal con-straints, such as export regulations, licensing agreements, or funding agency restrictions, may prevent artifact release. Restrictive publication page limits further impede detailed methodological reporting: 50 of 77 papers span 8 pages or fewer. Even with better descriptions, shared tools, and public benchmarks, the absence of agreed-upon ground truth defini-tions and evaluation metrics remains a fundamental challenge, given the diversity of reverse engineering goals [6, 69, 122]. 

## 4 Evaluation of HRE Artifacts (RQ3) 

Building on the challenges identified in Section 3, particu-larly those related to reproducibility and cumulative research progress, we analyze the artifacts accompanying HRE pub-lications. Out of the 187 papers, 31 ( 17 % ) report providing publicly available artifacts. As two papers reference the same artifact, this corresponds to 30 unique artifacts. Despite a recent increase in artifact publication— 23 % for papers pub-lished between 2021 and 2025 compared to 9 % between 1986 and 2020—the overall integration of artifacts into HRE re-search remains limited. We summarize our findings in Table 4. Detailed per-artifact evaluation notes, including documented evaluation steps, encountered issues, and mitigation attempts, are available as part of our open-science materials. 

Availability. Of the 30 artifacts identified in our corpus, 24 were fully available, one was partially available, and five were inaccessible. Permanent storage was used infrequently, appearing only 6 times; however, its first use in 2023 may in-dicate a shift toward more durable artifact hosting. This trend is encouraging, as three artifacts hosted on non-permanent websites are no longer accessible. While 23 artifacts were stored using a single method, either permanent storage, non-permanent storage, or access upon request, two artifacts were distributed across multiple storage types. One of these re-lied on non-permanent hosting and on-request access, but no response was received when access was requested. 11 Functionality. Across the 25 available artifacts, quality ranged from no usable guidance (2 artifacts with no or non-applicable instructions) to major documentation gaps (4 ar-tifacts, e. g., with hard-coded absolute paths, no execution instructions, or a missing dependency list). The remaining 19 artifacts described a workable “path to first success,” with only minor friction such as adding a missing package, a build flag, or adjusting a configuration or file path. In terms of completeness, all artifacts were fully delivered, except for one, where part of the artifact had to be requested, but was not provided. Regarding exercisability, our evaluation of the 20 available tools showed that all were executable, but only 6 could be executed without issues. For 14 artifacts, execu-tion typically required adjustments to the setup—often due to incomplete or unclear documentation—resulting in a trial-and-error approach. All encountered issues, along with the corresponding resolution strategies, are documented for each artifact individually in our open science materials. Given our best-effort evaluation without author support, these results are a good approximation of the artifacts’ real-world usability. 

Reproducibility. Of 18 papers with available artifacts that include concrete, testable results and provide the correspond-ing executable tools, we could reproduce some key results of seven papers. The remaining attempts failed primarily due to missing evaluation materials (e. g., required binaries, netlists, bitstreams, IC images, or labeled datasets) or because the tools necessary for reproducing the results were not provided (e. g., for computing the error rate between segmented images and labeled data, or for calculating evaluation scores). While we acknowledge that not all papers in our corpus contain verifiable claims, we consider the number of 7 reproducible papers (4 %) out of 187 to be exceptionally low. 

## 5 Opportunities for Scalable HRE Research 

Collectively, the findings reveal substantial overlap in obsta-cles across IC , FPGA , and netlist reverse engineering. Mit-igating three systemic, cross-cutting challenges would sig-nificantly reduce the difficulty of addressing the localized technical problems identified in RQ2: (i) limited reproducibil-ity and reusability due to the scarcity of shared artifacts and usable tooling, (ii) limited comparability due to the absence of standardized benchmarks, ground truth, and evaluation met-rics, and (iii) limited legal clarity and clearance, which con-strain data sharing and collaboration. Together, they present clear opportunities for enabling more rigorous, cumulative, and scalable HRE research. We structure our recommenda-tions around these three opportunities and outline stakeholder-specific actions to address each challenge. 

Opportunity I: Value Reproducibility and Reusability. 

Many contributions remain difficult to reproduce and build upon: code is unpublished or fragile, and pipelines are be-spoke. Consequently, technical approaches are often incom-plete and localized, limiting generalizability. We attribute this to structural factors, including missing academic incen-tives, limited awareness, insufficient industry cooperation, and legal constraints on publication. This situation is partic-ularly harmful in HRE , where intermediate results naturally feed later pipeline stages. HRE lacks broadly adopted, end-to-end community frameworks into which new methods can be integrated, comparable to the rich open-source ecosys-tem in Software Reverse Engineering ( SRE ) with tools like Ghidra. Early promising efforts, such as the Degate IC reverse engineering suite, have largely stagnated [41]. Meanwhile, FPGA -focused tools like Project X-Ray [54] remain limited to a narrow device family. For netlist reverse engineering, HAL [58] represents a notable exception and has gained some traction [159], yet it still lacks widespread adoption. 

Venues and Academic Incentives. Improving reproducibil-ity and reuse requires aligning community incentives with artifact-centric research practices. While USENIX Security has demonstrated effective mechanisms through mandatory artifact evaluation, artifact publication and long-term archival should become the default for all HRE -relevant venues, with explicit exceptions. The community should also explicitly re-ward engineering work through tool and infrastructure tracks and through mechanisms promoting long-term maintenance, e. g., recognition for impactful and living artifacts [1]. These priorities should extend beyond venues and be reflected in Ph.D. program design, hiring, and funding decisions, treating tools and curated datasets as first-class research outputs. 

Individual Researchers. Within such incentive-aligned envi-ronments, researchers should select venues whose page limits do not impede reporting methodological details sufficient for replication. When publishing artifacts in—and beyond— HRE , researchers should package pipelines in containers or virtual machines and pin dependency versions to ensure long-term reproducibility. Methods should be implemented as modular components with clear interfaces and adapters to shared frameworks wherever possible, enabling reuse beyond a single paper. Furthermore, all inputs and parameters used to generate published results should be clearly documented. 

Industry and Funders. Industry can accelerate reproducibil-ity by contributing engineering time to extend and maintain open-source HRE tooling. Funders of academic research projects should consider supporting multi-year maintenance grants for open HRE infrastructure that focus on community-available tooling rather than purely on algorithmic novelty. 

Opportunity II: Ensure Rigorous Comparability. Even when authors publish functional and exercisable algorithms, progress is often hard to judge: papers evaluate on incom-parable targets, rely on private datasets, and report metrics that do not translate across methods. A major reason for this is the lack of community-wide benchmarks, which prevents 12 consistent, meaningful comparison. To serve as dependable, shared evaluation targets, future benchmarks should (i) be legally cleared, (ii) provide explicit ground truth where possi-ble, and (iii) use agreed-upon metrics tied to practical utility. Examples include: IC images paired with GDSII and netlists; FPGA bitstreams paired with original netlists; and netlists paired with gate-level labels, modular structure, RTL (where feasible), and design specifications. Because hardware manu-facturing and the threats addressed by defensive HRE evolve rapidly, benchmarks must be treated as living infrastructure with versioning and long-term curation. 

Venues and Community Norms. Venues should recognize benchmark creation and maintenance as an integral part of research impact. Especially for widely cited claims, replica-tion and re-evaluation on contemporary technologies should be encouraged. Inspiration for organizing such efforts may be drawn from fields such as machine learning, where sus-tained, community-driven benchmark projects have become central to progress [20, 73]. For benchmark creation in HRE ,initiatives such as ICCAD’s annual CAD Contest [75], which has included netlist reverse engineering problems in past edi-tions, could serve as a valuable starting point. Existing efforts like TrustHUB [174] provide centralized dataset hosting, but broader community engagement and sustained curation ap-pear limited [91], highlighting the need for clear governance, reliable ground truth, and ongoing maintenance. 

Individual Researchers. Researchers should provide evalu-ation scripts and metric implementations and, where bench-marks exist, evaluate their tools and techniques on those shared targets using multiple metrics. To support reproducibil-ity and reuse, publications should explicitly state assumptions, describe limitations, and highlight potential failure scenarios. 

Industry. Creating and maintaining realistic benchmarks— especially for IC and netlist reverse engineering—often ex-ceeds what academic groups can achieve. Industry is uniquely positioned to supply legally cleared reference designs with verified ground truth and support long-term curation, includ-ing updates, governance, and documentation. Such challenge datasets, reflecting modern constraints, can accelerate re-search into supply chain security and defensive HRE. 

Opportunity III: Provide Legal Clearance and Clarity. 

Legal constraints and uncertainties are a structural inhibitor of HRE : they limit dataset and tool distribution, and lead to target and workflow descriptions that are insufficient for repli-cation. Constraints arise from licensing terms, non-disclosure agreements, and export-control regulations. For instance, open-source IC reverse engineering frameworks may be clas-sified as dual-use technologies under European Union ( EU )export regulations, requiring explicit export approval [52, 53]. IP concerns introduce further uncertainty about which results may be published. We argue that such restrictions are increas-ingly misaligned with contemporary hardware security needs. For instance, this misalignment manifests in workforce devel-opment: unlike SRE , HRE lacks a broad talent pipeline due to the high barrier to entry [178]. Public research and education are therefore among the few viable entry points for training practitioners, necessitating appropriate legal protection. 

Venues and Institutions. To reduce legal uncertainty for individual researchers, venues and institutions should provide clear guidance on legally constrained artifacts, including rec-ommended sanitization techniques and minimum disclosure requirements. Well-resourced institutions could also support researchers by offering legal consultation, handling approvals, or negotiating exemptions where possible, thereby enabling broader participation in HRE research. 

Industry. Industry can reduce friction without compromis-ing IP by offering research-friendly licensing terms for bench-mark use and publication. 

Government and Regulators. Governments should reassess legal barriers and export restrictions that disproportionately impede HRE , support broad access to publicly funded re-search outputs, and fund public-private mechanisms for legally sound sharing and long-term infrastructure mainte-nance. While both HRE and SRE involve dual-use tech-nology, the SRE ecosystem demonstrates that government-backed open-source tools can flourish (e. g., Ghidra). A key goal is to avoid concentrating HRE capability within a small set of privileged actors, which would reduce transparency and weaken independent verification. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This work systematizes over two decades of peer-reviewed Hardware Reverse Engineering ( HRE ) research, consolidat-ing fragmented techniques and terminology across 187 publi-cations spanning IC , FPGA , and netlist reverse engineering. We provide a shared reference for the community, offering an overview of current HRE practices that enable critical secu-rity applications, including design verification, supply-chain assurance, and vulnerability discovery. Our analysis also reveals a concerning pattern: despite substantial technical advances, progress often remains difficult to reproduce or build upon. With only 4% of studies providing reproducible artifacts, much of the field continues to rely on effectively opaque implementations rather than shared progress. To address persisting technical and organizational chal-lenges in the field, we suggest coordinated action: academic venues and programs should reward artifact creation and main-tenance, individual researchers should publish reusable and well-documented methods, industry should provide legally cleared reference designs and maintain standardized bench-marks, and regulators should reassess legal barriers that im-pede open research. Our work constitutes a systematization and critical reflection on existing publicly available research. It does not propose new reverse-engineering techniques, disclose novel vulner-abilities, or lower the barrier to conducting attacks beyond what is already described in the surveyed literature. The study involves no human participants and does not collect, process, or analyze personal data. Throughout this work, we respect the rights of authors and publishers of the analyzed publi-cations and comply with applicable copyright and licensing requirements. Given the descriptive and reflective nature of our contribution, we believe that this work raises no material ethical concerns that would preclude its publication. Nonetheless, we acknowledge the dual - use nature of Hard-ware Reverse Engineering ( HRE ) itself. It can support legiti-mate goals such as vulnerability discovery, design verification, supply-chain assurance, and patent infringement analysis, but may also be abused to extract proprietary design informa-tion or facilitate hardware-level attacks. In the following, we provide a stakeholder analysis of HRE , discussing each stakeholder’s interests in HRE and how they may be affected. 

Chip Designers could use HRE to verify that their IC de-signs and schematics are correctly implemented and were not maliciously modified during manufacturing. Furthermore, they can use HRE to detect IP infringements by other vendors or to perform competitor analysis. At the same time, chip designers may be negatively affected if adversaries use HRE to extract their proprietary designs to conduct IP theft or to insert hardware Trojans during manufacturing. 

Manufacturers such as foundries and their fabs can use HRE to verify third-party IP blocks or to debug complex man-ufacturing issues. However, manufacturers may also abuse HRE to extract IP from customers or competitors, or to intro-duce malicious modifications during fabrication. Conversely, manufacturers may themselves be harmed if their proprietary manufacturing process or IP are reverse engineered by others. 

System integrators , who purchase chips to incorporate them into larger systems, may rely on HRE to verify the trustwor-thiness of third-party components, particularly in safety- or security-critical applications. This can help identify counter-feit, vulnerable, or trojanized hardware. 

Government agencies , including intelligence, military, and law enforcement organizations, may apply HRE in both of-fensive and defensive contexts: to extract data or exploit hardware vulnerabilities, assess adversarial systems, verify the integrity of critical components, or evaluate proprietary technology in the context of national security. Because of the prohibitive cost of some HRE steps, particularly those related to IC sample preparation and imaging, state-level actors likely possess the most advanced capabilities in practice. Increased open research in HRE and public availability of tools may reduce the exclusivity of such capabilities. Given that agen-cies often seek to keep such capabilities secret to maintain a strategic advantage, they may attempt to limit or counteract open research and public dissemination of HRE tools. 

Academic researchers engaged in HRE aim to understand attacker capabilities, uncover vulnerabilities, and develop ver-ification and assurance techniques. Open publication of such research can strengthen security by enabling independent scrutiny and reproducibility. However, academic researchers may face legal, contractual, or political pressures that con-strain disclosure, limit access to hardware, or discourage pub-lication due to national security or IP infringement concerns. 

Regulators and certification bodies , such as standardization organizations and lawmakers, have a strong interest in HRE to assess the safety, security, and compliance of hardware used in critical infrastructure and government domains. In practice, HRE supports independent certification and helps enforce trust and accountability across global supply chains. 

The general public —as end users of hardware and ben-eficiaries of critical digital infrastructure—is indirectly but significantly affected by HRE . Undisclosed vulnerabilities, counterfeit components, or implanted hardware Trojans can undermine safety, privacy, and trust. At the same time, respon-sible and transparent HRE research can improve long-term security and resilience by enabling earlier detection of risks. Overall, ethical HRE research requires balancing openness with legitimate state and commercial interests, recognizing its dual-use nature, and considering how publication would affect commercial actors, governments, and the public. We believe that carefully conducted public HRE research is essential for improving hardware security, provided that ethical risks are explicitly acknowledged and appropriately mitigated.

## B Codebooks 

Table 2: Codebook used to identify the core topics of the Hardware Reverse Engineering ( HRE ) process addressed by each paper. Exactly one primary code is assigned with arbitrarily many secondary codes. 

Code Topics Full Process Full HRE process or vast parts of it. May only cover IC, FPGA, or netlist. 

IC 

Sample Preparation Physical ablation of material, such as depackaging, delayering, backside thinning, and cross-section cuts. Imaging Image acquisition, including optical, SEM, TEM, X-ray, and micro-CT imaging methods. Image Analysis & Netlist Extraction Analysis of IC images or GDSII files, e. g., stitching, stacking, segmentation, template matching, and ML algorithms, for netlist extraction or region-of-interest identification. 

FPGA 

Bitstream Extraction Recovery of a (plaintext or encrypted) bitstream from an FPGA. Bitstream Format RE Reverse engineering the unknown format of an FPGA bitstream. Bitstream Conversion Converting a bitstream into a gate-level netlist using an existing bitstream format database. Exclusion: Bitstream Encryption Attacking and/or breaking bitstream encryption. Exclusion: Bitstream Manipulation Manipulating existing bitstreams based on a known bitstream format. 

Netlist 

Control Path / FSM RE Control-path and FSM reverse engineering, including finding control structures, FSM logic, and reconstruction of FSM transition graphs. Also includes algorithms that simply classify gates as control-path logic. Data Path Data-path reverse engineering, including register recovery, arithmetic logic detection and analysis, and general data-path logic analysis. Also includes algorithms that simply classify gates as data-path logic. Partitioning & Module Identification Partitioning and the functional identification of previously isolated modules. Structural Methods Structural analysis, e. g., based on the netlist graph representation. Functional Methods Functional netlist analysis,e. g., using Boolean functions, SMT/SAT solvers, Boolean equivalence matching, and simulation. Transistor Level Transistor-level netlist reverse engineering. Exclusion: Anti-RE Techniques Defenses Techniques to hamper HRE on either the physical or logical level. Attacks Attacking or breaking techniques that hamper HRE on either the physical or logical level. Exclusion: Hardware Trojans Detection Techniques for hardware trojan detection on physical or logical level. Design Hardware trojan design and insertion. Exclusion: PCB PCB reverse engineering. Exclusion: Hardware Design Hardware design or manufacturing aspects that are not clearly related to HRE or hardware obfuscation. Exclusion: Side Channel & Fault Analyses Side-channel attacks, including optical probing, and fault analysis. Exclusion: Minor Special Topics Not captured by any other topic, but loosely related to HRE, e. g., PUFs, open-source hardware, split manufacturing, etc.
